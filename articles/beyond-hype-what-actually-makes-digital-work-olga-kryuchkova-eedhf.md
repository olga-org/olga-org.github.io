### I. Origins & Background

Olga K.: Jarmo, can you please tell us a bit about your career path and experience you gained? If we rewound to the very start of your career, what belief about digitalization did you hold back then that you’ve completely changed your mind about today?

Jarmo Pulkkinen: I graduated with a Master's degree in Administrative Sciences from the University of Vaasa in the late 1990s. Around the same time, I co-founded Web Fellows Oy, a company focused on digital production. Digitalization was in its early stages then, especially with the rapid development of the Internet. While technologies have evolved significantly, I believe the fundamental idea of digitalization has remained the same: **beyond technology itself, we need a deep understanding of how people and organizations actually function**.

**Olga:** You’ve worked with 300+ organizations — was there one early failure or messy project that quietly shaped how you lead today more than any success?

**Jarmo:** No major catastrophe comes immediately to mind, but the most common challenge I've seen in digitalization projects, across both public and private sectors, is **over-optimism about timelines**. Because we're not just dealing with devices and software but with human collaboration, schedules must be planned realistically. Another crucial lesson has been about **leadership and ownership**: projects need strong leadership both on the client organization's side and the system provider's side. This challenge exists in organizations of all sizes.

**Olga:** How did your PhD in Administrative Sciences at University of Lapland and change the way you look at technology decisions compared to purely technical AI leaders?

**Jarmo:** The biggest aha moment has been understanding systemic complexity; recognizing that things are deeply interconnected and that proving clear cause-and-effect relationships is genuinely challenging. I've developed a certain humility toward research and a better understanding of the limits of my own knowledge. It's been wonderful to work alongside people who are smarter than me.

**Olga:** Before “AI” became a buzzword, what problem were you already trying to solve that now feels surprisingly ahead of its time?

**Jarmo:** Since the early 2000s, when we were among the first to bring email marketing to Finland, I've been advocating for measurement and evaluation. Digital environments offer tremendous potential for measurement whether it's clicks, dwell time, functionality, or qualitative data. There's still significant work to be done in this area, both in the public and private sectors.

**Olga:** If someone shadowed you for a week in your first digitalization job, what would surprise them most compared to your work now?

**Jarmo:** My first projects as a project manager involved creating websites for Finlandia Vodka Ltd and Volvo Cars Project management itself hasn't changed much, but remote meetings, cloud services, and leveraging AI have brought significantly more efficiency and transformed working methods.

### II. The Idea & Innovation

**Olga:** What’s an idea about digital transformation in public health that sounds counterintuitive but you’re convinced is true?

**Jarmo:** Previous research has focused heavily on digital services for residents and clients. I believe we should research and develop the digitalization of service production itself more; how we could better utilize devices, software, and digital materials in the actual delivery of services.

**Olga:** When you evaluate a new AI idea, what signals tell you “this is hype” vs. “this could actually change lives”?

**Jarmo:** If you can't state in one sentence what problem or challenge you're solving, it’s a red flag. **Technology itself is not the goal, it's a tool**.

**Olga:** If a small wellbeing services county asked you for one “minimum viable AI” experiment tomorrow, what would you choose and why?

**Jarmo:** I would create basic guidelines for using generative AI tools (like Copilot and ChatGPT). Which tools can be used, for what purposes, etc. Then I would share experiences from pioneering employees. These initiatives cost almost nothing.

**Olga:** In your ecosystem research, knowledge sharing was the strongest participation driver — why do you think “learning together” beats funding or tech in motivating organizations?

**Jarmo:** Many actions are needed both within organizations and nationally. But lack of knowledge is also the primary barrier at the employee level.

**Olga:** What constraint — regulation, funding, or skills — has paradoxically sparked the most creative solutions in your experience?

**Jarmo:** When it comes to basic skills, peer support has emerged to compensate for inadequate support from employer organizations.

### III. Digitalization in Finland & the National AI Ecosystem

**Olga:** Your study identified five mechanisms behind ecosystem participation — knowledge, resources, legitimacy, regulation, and societal impact. Which one is most underestimated by leaders right now and why?

**Jarmo:** There's a lot of talk about legislation, and for good reason. Similarly about skills development and lack of resources. But less about legitimacy and societal impact, perhaps because they don't really have clear ownership in Finland right now.

**Olga:**  With 160+ organizations joining early on, what invisible “glue” actually keeps such a diverse ecosystem working together instead of fragmenting?

**Jarmo:** The enthusiasm of pioneering employees! **Organizations don't collaborate; people do**.

 **Olga:**  You recommend moving from ministerial-led governance toward distributed stakeholder leadership — what risks and opportunities come with letting go of central control?

**Jarmo:** The risk, of course, is erosion of credibility when the ministry doesn't legitimize the activity. The opportunity is concrete projects.

**Olga:**  If Finland wanted to become a global model for public-sector AI, what one structural change would have the highest leverage?

**Jarmo:** Genuine close cooperation between municipalities and wellbeing services counties, along with national projects.

**Olga:**  After working nationwide, where do you see the biggest digital maturity gap: between regions, sectors, or professions?

**Jarmo:** In Finland, digitalization is viewed positively at all levels. There's also substantial technical expertise at many levels. Perhaps the biggest differences are in organizational readiness. Organizations that invest in empowering pioneers, infrastructure, data management, inter-organizational collaboration, and employee support will succeed in the future as well.

### IV. Tech, AI, Data & Ethics — Lessons from Real Projects

**Olga:**  Your challenge study shows the biggest barriers aren’t technology or data, but competence and organizational capacity — why do leaders still default to buying tools first?

**Jarmo:** Because there's more experience with that approach. It's much easier to buy a tool than to think through processes or skills development.

**Olga:**  When staff resist AI, is it really about ethics and privacy — or about time pressure and change fatigue?

**Jarmo:** This varies between sectors. For example, in municipal technical services, people worry about whether AI-generated answers are correct. In social and healthcare, people don't know which tools to use or how to use them.

**Olga:**  You found that legislation like the EU AI Act isn’t the core problem, but unclear guidance is. What would “good guidance” actually look like in practice?

**Jarmo:** Guidance must be implemented both at the organizational level and at the employee level (i.e., role/task-based).

**Olga:**   If an AI system makes a mistake in public healthcare, who should truly be accountable — the developer, the organization, or the policy framework?

**Jarmo:** Wellbeing services counties must conduct information security and privacy assessments for each implementation before deployment. These define such matters. But responsibility lies with everyone: system providers, the organization, and the employee.

**Olga:**   Have you ever deliberately said, “We should not automate this,” even when AI was possible? What made you draw that ethical line?

Ethical questions are important but very challenging. For example, we can't say with certainty whether existing algorithms contain biases. If a solution endangers client or patient safety, that's definitely a line.

### V. Vision, Future & Collaboration

**Olga:** Personally, what would make you say in 2030: “Yes — this ecosystem actually improved societal wellbeing, not just digitized processes”?

**Jarmo:** I would ask both employees and clients/patients. The experience of benefit is at least as important as measurements using various metrics, such as economic growth.

**Olga:** What type of partner do you feel Finland’s AI health ecosystem needs more of right now: startups, researchers, policymakers, or something unexpected?

**Jarmo:** Good products first and foremost! From these we get benefits, experiences, and economic growth.

**Olga:** If you could run one bold national AI experiment with no bureaucracy for six months, what would you test first?

**Jarmo:** I would choose a challenge or problem whose solution would help as many professionals as possible nationally; whether nurses, doctors, planners, or teachers.

**Olga:** Where do you feel you personally still need support or complementary skills to scale your impact?

**Jarmo:** I understand very little about how machine learning actually works. Understanding how technology functions isn't the most essential skill for my work, but the topic interests me personally.

**Olga:** What type of collaboration would make you say, “This is exactly the AI ecosystem Finland has been missing”?

**Jarmo:** Projects involving multiple public organizations that could result in a nationally deployed solution benefiting a large number of professionals and clients. We would need a national database where one could search for what AI solutions already exist and what impacts and effectiveness they have achieved.
