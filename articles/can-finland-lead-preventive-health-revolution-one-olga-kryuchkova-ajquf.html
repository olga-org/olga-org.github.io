I. Origins & Founder’s Background

Olga K.: What personal or professional experience pushed you from patient files to product development?

Noah Petermann: I was on the other side of the table, working as a VC and seeing all the deep-tech moonshots - lab-grown organs, nanoscale drug delivery, etc. The science was thrilling, but every pitch ended with a footnote: “commercial in 12–15 years, regulation TBD. Meanwhile, I was self-hacking with basic blood panels and nutrition tweaks, only to discover that interpreting a ferritin result felt like reading hieroglyphs for most people".

That disconnect hit hard: we’re rewriting DNA while the average person doesn’t even know their vitamin-D level. I’m no physician, but years of diligence had trained me to digest biochemistry papers quickly, and the verdict was obvious – we can get people to live years longer with today’s knowledge. The bottleneck isn’t new science; it’s translating existing science into daily action.

So I swapped term sheets for Figma specs. Aniva was born to make biomarkers effortless and convert them into a living nutrition-and-supplement plan. Deep-tech breakthroughs will still change medicine, but only if we first fix the on-ramp to baseline health. That’s the gap we’d move everything to close.

II. The Idea & Innovation

Olga: What gap in the Finnish healthcare system did you notice that made you go: “This is insane—someone needs to fix this,” and then realized… it’s you? Which gaps in Finnish health system you can cover with your solution?

Noah: Let me start by saying that if I had a life threatening illness today, there wouldn’t be many places I’d rather be than Finland. Emergency care here really is excellent. But obviously, we struggle quite a bit with prevention and one of the best statistics for this is that Seventy-two percent of deaths are preventable, the average doctor visit clocks in at under five minutes, and healthcare spending has almost tripled in fifty years while life expectancy flat-lines. Those numbers are universal, but in Finland they feel especially jarring because the country already leads the world in wearables and digital records. We’re swimming in physiology data, yet people still enter the system only after something breaks.

That disconnect is the gap Aniva covers:

  • From signals to action Many Finns track sleep and HRV with Polar and ŌURA, but there’s no friction-free path to confirm what those blips mean biologically. We enrich recovery scores with a targeted blood panel you can book in sixty seconds - no queues, no faxed referrals.

  • From one-off visits to continuous care Five-minute consults are inevitable when physicians are buried in backlog. Our model off-loads routine optimization - vitamin, lipid, hormone tuning.- so GPs can focus on complex cases and chronic care. The everyday “sick-care” carousel slows down.

  • From static advice to adaptive plans Our biology isn’t static, so neither is our guidance. Biomarker trends feed an evolving nutrition, supplement and lifestyle protocol that updates as naturally as your playlist.

  • From confusion to clarity We curate only evidence-backed actions, stripping away the noise of contradictory headlines. The result is a single, credible roadmap people can actually follow.

Finland already proves you can industrialize personal data at scale. What’s missing is the last mile - turning that data into proactive, personalized healthcare that keeps you out of the waiting room in the first place. Seeing those numbers made us realize no one else was going to fix it for us. So we’re doing it.

III. Finnish Health Ecosystem and your solution

Olga: Have you noticed any differences between the Nordic countries and your home country, Austria, when it comes to finding partners and securing funding? How have these differences influenced your funding strategy and business model?

Noah: What really sets the Nordics apart isn’t an instant pipeline of cash - it’s the partnership fabric. Finland in particular has a ‘doors-unlocked’ mentality that puts a young founder two coffees away from people who’ve already built global health hits like Polar and ŌURA. When icons of consumer-grade physiology are willing to talk openly about their missteps and platform choices, you get a masterclass in go-to-market without spending a cent.

Student-run hubs – especially Kiuas , SILTA and Aaltoes - amplify that access. Show up at a Wednesday evening pitch clinic and you’re side-by-side with engineers and ex-Nokia product leads dissecting your API strategy. No résumé check, no warm intro - just shared curiosity and a bias to build.

Back home in Austria, the networks are tight and formal; partnerships often start after a lengthy credentials dance. Here, the default is: Co-create first, formalize later. That has reshaped our funding playbook. We’re prioritizing commercial pilots with ecosystem partners – stay tuned for what is to come here- before opening an equity round. Showing traction through partnerships lets us raise on evidence, not promises.

It also influences our business model. Rather than pouring money into heavy R&D, we plug into existing Nordic strengths: top-tier wearable data, nationwide e-health infrastructure, and a culture that rewards preventive care. That alignment keeps us capital-light, speeds our time to market, and, frankly, signals to investors that we know how to leverage the ecosystem instead of trying to out-spend it.

So the Nordic difference isn’t about larger cheques; it’s about a collaborative sandbox where proven operators and student talent help turn raw ideas into partner-ready products. For founders willing to learn in public, that environment is worth more than any early-stage term sheet.

IV. Tech, Data, AI & Ethics

Olga: AI and health? A cure or a curse? Where do you draw the ethical line with automation? Today it is not just a buzzing questions but the reality of AI implementation.

Noah: AI isn’t inherently a cure or a curse - it’s a powerful tool. The technology itself is neutral; the outcome depends on the intent and skills of the people using it.

At Aniva we treat AI as an amplifier, not a magic pill. It speeds up pattern-recognition, personalizes recommendations, and keeps us learning at the pace of new research - but it doesn’t replace human judgement. Our international advisory board - biochemists, bioinformaticians, practicing clinicians - keeps us honest by translating raw insights into actions that make biological sense.

So the real ethical line isn’t hidden in source code; it’s in the choices companies make about transparency, ownership, and accountability. Use AI to empower individuals and clinicians, and you extend health-span. Use it to cut corners or lock people out of their own data, and you erode trust. We’ve chosen the first path, and we believe most of the industry will too - because the market, regulators, and, frankly, human decency will demand it.

I guess to answer the question: we can see AI changing healthcare forever by freeing up clinicians time – but at the same time we need to – of course – also be mindful of data privacy which is why we always – besides all the other usual security measures – pseudonymize data before feeding it to our AI.

V. Vision & Future

Olga: Paint us a picture of Finland’s healthcare landscape 10 years from now—with and without your startup in it.

Noah:

Version A – Without Aniva Wearables are ubiquitous; many schoolkids have smart rings that logs sleep, HRV, even glucose. But the health system still runs on retro logic - data sits in silos, and you book a doctor only after something hurts. AI chatbots offer generic ‘drink more water’ tips, yet chronic disease keeps eating half the national budget. Average health-span edges up, but the gains are uneven: tech-savvy urbanites do well, rural regions lag behind, and waiting lists stubbornly persist. Finland is still good - just not as good as it could be.

Version B – With Aniva Preventive care is the default. Every Finn has a dynamic biomarker passport that updates in real time - from a €15 finger-prick kiosk at K-Market or a drive-thru lab slot you booked in sixty seconds. Your biological age, nutrient gaps, and cardio-metabolic risk stream straight into Kanta, where Aniva’s engine translates them into a living health plan: tweak vitamin-D in November darkness, dial back caffeine after three nights of poor HRV, auto-adjust supplements when your marathon block starts.

GPs spend their fifteen-minute consults solving complex puzzles, not chasing down iron deficiencies. Insurers reward prevention with lower premiums; employers see sick-days drop by double digits. The Ministry of Social Affairs proudly reports that the average Finn has gained five extra healthy years, while per-capita healthcare spending has finally flattened, have decreased and reached the majority of the population even in remote areas.

The Constant Even if Aniva fails spectacularly, the conversation we’re forcing about rethinking healthcare doesn’t disappear. People will keep demanding earlier answers, and someone else will fill the gaps. Preventive health is here to stay.

But we’d rather be the ones who make our version real - because the sooner Finland proves proactive care works at scale, the sooner the rest of Europe follows. If we do our job, a decade from now the idea of waiting until you’re sick will feel as outdated as fax machines. Finland’s rich legacy of technological innovation positions it as a dynamic high-tech hub—an engine for advancing not only healthcare, but a wide spectrum of cutting-edge industries shaping the future.
