I. Origins & Background — About the Team and How It Was Created

Olga K.: Ohe, please, tell us a bit more about your background, experience and interests?

How I Learned Finnish - with Ohe: So the raison d’etre of this project is that in 2022, I pretended not to speak English, so I could force myself to speak Finnish and finally reach fluency. My story before that was I moved to Finland when I was in 5th grade, studied in English-language schools till 9th grade and went to Ghana for high school and came back for university - which was also in English. I have been here in Finland ever since.  It was in 2021 after graduation when I decided that I can see how this lack of fluent Finnish is definitely going to limit how good of a life I could have in terms of my career, relationships, opportunities and just general surface area for luck.

My interests have been for the longest time, football and everything about that but, around 2020, I got into board games when someone introduced me to monopoly. I got hooked and spent a lot of money on building a board game collection and also organizing game nights for my friends. Then I also got introduced to Finnish baseball and got so into it that I decided to buy 15 gloves, balls bats and the entire setup so that I could organize games of that too. I have been doing that for about 3-4 summers now creating a lot of memories with a lot of people. My other interests worth mentioning are badminton and futsal.

Olga: How do the backgrounds of your collaborators or guests influence the direction of the podcast episodes?

Ohe: The backgrounds of the guests I interview on the interview series is very important because the idea is to show the audience that others from similar backgrounds to them have been able to achieve full fluency in Finnish.

 After I accomplished the fluency goal with the intensive one year experiment, I would often have conversations with people who would ask how I did it. After telling them, you would often hear excuses about how that wouldn’t work for them because of X or Y life situations. I heard that enough times that I naturally started to wonder, if achieving it was so rare and also how those others who had done the same were able to do it. That was when the concept of this project came to me and I decided to interview people of diverse backgrounds and life situations who had achieved the same goal so that the audience could most relate to those in similar life situations.

 So, I would say that the difference in background is vital for what I want to convey to the listener. This person was married or a student or a parent etc. during the time when they were trying to achieve this goal and for that reason, they are worth listening to. I ask the guests pretty much the same questions but the diversity of their background naturally guides the episode in the different directions that should be benefit the listener.

 II. The Idea & Innovation of the Podcast

Olga: Your content mixes storytelling with strategy — if you could describe this hybrid format as a genre (beyond “podcast”), what would you call it?

Ohe: So, I actually dislike calling it a podcast, even though I call it that just to get the point across faster. I prefer “limited interview series”. Limited because it doesn't have to go on forever; I want there to be enough examples that people can use to get a move on with working on the goal effectively. If it was a genre beyond podcasts, it would be lifestyle coaching or guidance. The reason being that the guest tells what they did on their day to day to get to the goal and the desired outcome is that the listener also does the same.

Olga: How might the podcast evolve if it weren’t bound by traditional episode length or format?

Ohe: The main limiter here is the resources since I do most of this by myself. If there wasn’t a resource and time problem, I would have it so that for every episode, there is a write-up that distills the core methods that the person used into an article. Then there would also be a page that lists all the specific tools that the person used with links to them. I would have further episodes taken out of the existing episodes that are only the methods that the person used. So those would be focused strictly on the methods used so that any busy listener could just get straight to the point.

 Olga: What’s the biggest creative tension you face between educational value and entertainment value — and how do you resolve it?

Ohe: I have had the issue of how long each interview should be. I keep it long because it can be boring to just listen to only educational nuggets. So I opted for the method where the guest can mix in as many of their own stories as possible. And naturally since the topic is how you learned the language and life is often not static, there is always a story to tell. Sometimes these stories become very tangential and unrelated but I opt to keep them in there for the entertainment value of the episodes.

I have heard for example the story of Jojo who was in a refugee camp and the Finnish class was full but he really really wanted to join. So he would stand at the door and listen to the class. It got to some point where when the teacher asked a question, he was the only one with his hands up. So eventually the teacher relented and let him join. Another interesting story that comes to mind at this point of my writing is how Hamed’s story unfolded coupled with how funny and how good of a story teller he is. It would be a waste to just let that go and be formulaic about how he learned the language. Or the issue that Anita faced regarding losing custody of her kids which was the genesis of her learning the language. So the stories add entertainment value but they also are related to learning the language.

 III. Relevance of Podcast & Finnish Life

Olga: From your interviews, what’s one Finnish cultural habit that actually accelerates language learning — but foreigners rarely notice at first?

Ohe: Sisu and not complicating things would be very beneficial in making things happen.

Olga: In your view, what’s the most powerful non-language benefit of learning Finnish — something that surprises even advanced learners?

Ohe: There is a concept that I have come across called – increasing the surface area for luck. Living in Finland, all things being equal, there is such a huge increase in the surface area for luck that a person experiences just by being able to speak the language. How often you can connect to other humans, navigate situations, overcome obstacles is greatly improved by the process of learning Finnish. Another thing that occurs is that, to learn Finnish, you utilize any excess capacity you have after work or school or taking care of your other obligations. Which then tells you that you do have extra capacity to achieve things beyond the mundane. That you have some extra horsepower to deploy in life. Once you realize that, you can apply that to many endeavors; on extracurricular achievements. You can achieve a lot of good by doing the same for other things because learning Finnish is something that takes a lot of time.

IV. Tech, AI, Data & Ethics Reflections and Experience of Use in Projects

Olga: How might emerging tech change the way people learn Finnish in the next 5 years — beyond apps and translation tools?

Ohe: With the way things are, and developing further due to Artificial intelligence, there is a lower barrier to practice. Practicing writing can be done in a way that you prompt an LLM chat for a question, write the answer yourself in your target language and then ask it to check the correctness. There is a much faster feedback loop than I had in 2022 when I decided to learn the language. I had to ask friends or my then partner to correct me. With regards to new words that I saw in books, I would manually put them into excel and then go search news articles and online for example sentences – this could take 2 hours for just 7 words because the sentences had to be suitably understandable for me to find useful. Now I can just use artificial intelligence to generate suitably easy or difficult example sentences involving that specific word so I can practice its usage. All this is to say that it can make it a lot easier. However, there is something odd about humans and how much struggle affects us. I don’t know if the friction I encountered helped in the learning process itself or not but that is something I will leave to the experts.

V. Vision & Future of How I Learned Finnish

Olga: If you had unlimited resources, what’s one ambitious extension of the podcast that you would launch immediately?

Ohe: The playful thing would definitely be to make copies of the Abraham Maslow joke hoodie that I recently commissioned for myself and some of my guests for anyone who had managed to learn Finnish as an adult. On a more serious note, I would also make sure that there is access to people who have managed to learn Finnish as adults for any new learners. I believe most learners do not have access to people who have learned Finnish as adults. A new learner cannot distinguish between someone who speaks very fluently and someone who is a native speaker. So even if they had someone like that in their life, since they don’t know the difference between native and learned, they can't even tell who has been through the journey they are about to go through. Thus, they wouldn’t know to seek their advice. So workshops where learners and those who have learned could mingle and bounce ideas off each other and motivate each other would be incredible.

Olga: What partnerships or support structures would help the Finnish-learning ecosystem grow outside your own audience?

Ohe: One of my guests, Erik, had this great idea for  helping learners based on how he learned. He would buy lunch for someone to speak Finnish with him for an hour – usually a retired person. So an environment that would put older or available people together with learners to speak Finnish would be great. It would help the loneliness issue and help people to practice Finnish.

 Olga: How do you envision the role of How I Learned Finnish in Finland’s broader cultural or immigration landscape in 2030?

Ohe: The goal of this is to become the de facto tool that everyone uses when they are moving to Finland. That way they have first hand accounts of how people have learned Finnish and then they can go about the journey in a better way than they would otherwise have gone about it.

Olga: What’s one support you wish more people — brands, institutions, learners — knew how to give you?

Ohe: Spreading the word! There is so much news about negative stuff happening in the world. Sometimes there are articles about restaurants and about art exhibitions etc. I feel this project could be a prime target for some media coverage in a similar manner. It would help a lot of people and help Finland as well. This being the go-to resource would make things for new learners that much smoother. So, native speakers knowing about this to recommend as well as learners knowing about this to share with others would be fantastic.

Olga: What kind of collaborators do you think could bring the most value to the project?

Ohe: I think some media coverage would be great for making this the go-to resource in the minds of the people. Also, universities and other learning institutions pointing their students to this resource - i.e. our website https://www.howilearnedfinnish.fi/ would be mutually beneficial to the students, the institutions and make the project feel worthwhile.
