# [From Student in Italy to professional in Finland: How one woman 'transformed' her old identity to achieve true independence](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/from-student-italy-professional-finland-how-one-woman-olga-kryuchkova-phevc)

*Published on 2025-12-16 15:00*

## I. Background & Identity. Before Finland: Italy

Olga K. : Erika, can you tell us a little about your life and experience before Finland?

Erika Barbara Bottacci: Before Finland, I was living in Italy and was 23 years old when I moved. I was a physics student at the Università del Salento (Lecce). I had a very basic student life, heavily engaged in political conversations and socially active, spending time socializing, learning, reading, and focusing on my personal development. I had a dream of traveling the world, doing research, and exploring different remote areas.

**Olga:**  What brought you to Finland, and what were your first impressions? Any culture shocks?

**Erika:** The Erasmus+ program brought me here. I had a choice between Granada, Spain, for optics and Lahti, Finland, for environmental technology. Lahti felt more in tune with me, even though I had never heard of Finland before. I arrived on January 5th, 2015 in the evening, with about 2 meters of snow and - 20 degrees Celsius, which I was not prepared for. I was shocked by the Finnish language; when I first heard it, I panicked, realizing I couldn't use my existing language knowledge to build survival skills.

After the Erasmus program, I immediately realized how safe and secure this country is. I felt that **women are emancipated and treated fairly equally to men**. I **cherished the clean air and water**. I perceived that here, I could truly do something meaningful with my life. These factors solidified my decision to stay.

![Photo by Olga K.](/assets/Publications/erika1.jpg)

**Olga:**  What’s one everyday challenge in Finland that no one prepared you for?

**Erika:** I was not prepared for the challenges of integration in certain areas, particularly navigating the less diverse social environments. For the first time, I felt I was perceived as part of a minority background. **Navigating this challenge of difference and the lack of cultural awareness in some settings helped me become more resilient, ultimately strengthening my core values, morale, and ethics.**

**Olga:**   What’s a small tradition or habit from your home country that you’ve kept alive in Finland?

**Erika:** I have kept alive the element of **relying on social groups and caring for others**, which goes back to my Italian cultural background.

**Olga:**  How has living in Finland reshaped the way you see yourself as a woman and a professional?

**Erika:** Living in Finland has been essential for shaping me as a woman and a professional. As an Italian woman from a collectivistic culture, my existence initially relied on the social group. When I came to Finland, those concepts were destroyed. I reinvented myself through self-reflection and navigating cultural differences. Before turning 30, I felt I had finally reached the "state of adulthood", becoming **less reliant on a social group and more reliant on myself**, which made me much more firm, strong, independent, and confident.

As a **professional, I learned to be more practical**. Initially, I was idealistic, but in my fight for survival. Later I learned that dreams and ideas are not enough; you need a practical and realistic plan. **I focused on developing skills and competences that I could see applied in real life and that would bring rewards, establish roots, and create a solid structure for my professional life.**

### II. Challenges & Resilience. In Finland: Lahti, Lappeenranta, Helsinki

**Olga:**  What has been one of the biggest challenges you’ve faced as a working woman in Finland?

**Erika:** I am proud and grateful for the high level of security for working women in Finland. My main challenges stemmed from being an international newcomer and a young professional. Early in my career, I navigated expectations that required a significant additional effort compared to my Finnish colleagues.

**Olga:**  How did you overcome it — and what did it teach you about yourself?

**Erika:** I quickly learned the **vital importance of being proactive and informed about my professional rights**. I used that experience as a catalyst to thoroughly study Finnish employment law, which helped me recognize, define, and uphold healthy professional boundaries going forward. I learned to effectively advocate for myself, set clear boundaries, and ensure sustainable working conditions, which ultimately taught me the importance of maintaining professional excellence without compromising my well-being.

**Olga:**   Have you ever faced a situation where “silence” or “quiet culture” in Finland felt like a barrier — and how did you navigate it?

**Erika:** Initially, the quiet culture frustrated me. However, I learned that silence is just the surface; once you enter a person's life, you find a culture of calmness and balance. I came to understand that **people who try to fill silence are often not at ease with themselves**, but **Finns are deep thinkers** who are content with themselves and their thoughts. After 10 years, I've welcomed and adopted this culture, only speaking when I have something meaningful to say. I navigated it by learning empathetic and compassionate communication in my closest relationships. In turn, this approach helped my Finnish friends express more complex thoughts out loud, resulting in much stronger bonds built on mutual trust and respect.

**Olga:**  What was your most surprising mistake or failure here that later became a strength?

**Erika:** My constant need to prove my worth and impress people around me was an unhealthy cycle. Being aware of this became a strength because it taught me to be more compassionate with myself, to be present in life and enjoy its simplicity, to let things be, and recognize that I don't need to prove anything to anybody.

**Olga:**   What Finnish system (bureaucracy, work culture, education) was hardest to understand at first, and how did you learn to adapt?

**Erika:** The hardest system for me was healthcare. I arrived 10 years ago and, at the time, accessing care in smaller towns like Lahti was challenging due to the limited availability of services in English. I often experienced difficulty navigating the appointment booking process, with calls frequently being disconnected when I requested to switch languages, which left me without alternative recommendations or support. This exposed a significant inflexibility in the system for non-Finnish speakers who urgently needed care.

After the Erasmus was completed and decided to stay in Finland, I was working for two years before restarting my studies. The support arrived only then, in 2018 and almost three years after I had moved to the country, when I started the studies again. The help I received from the student nurses was essential. It was only thanks to the healthcare support granted by the education system that I could finally access health care services in English. If I would have decided to not start studying, I would have had to wait much longer, and I cannot imagine how my health would have been or where my life would be right now if I would not have had that access.

I didn't truly adapt to the lack of flexibility in the system; I am still struggling and feel frustrated at times. I now luckily have access to healthcare provided by the employer and of course can consider seeking specialist advice also outside of Finland if needed.

### III. Inspiration & Role Models

**Olga:**   If you could have coffee with any woman from history or today, who would it be — and what would you ask her?

**Erika:** I would like to have coffee with **Marie Curie**, the first woman to win a Nobel Prize. I would ask her: "How did you manage to navigate in a male-dominated world, what were your worries, and how did you overcome the challenges?". I would also ask how she feels about being recognized in this world.

**Olga:**  Do you think role models should always be “perfect,” or can imperfection be inspiring too?

**Erika:** I don't think humans are perfect, and therefore a role model cannot be perfect. I believe the real beauty of people comes from their imperfections. Perfection, to me, does not exist and honestly should not be cherished because it is neither human nor real.

**Imperfection is actually the key to human beauty**. It is what is misunderstood, it is the pain, and it is the gaps we want to fill that truly drive people forward and motivate them to grow and develop. Seeing someone who is successful but has clearly navigated and overcome their flaws is far more relatable and inspiring than an image of untouchable perfection.

**Olga:**  What’s the best piece of advice you’ve received from another woman that you still live by?

**Erika:** The best piece of advice is that there is only one person that will cherish you, be with you, and support you forever, and that is yourself. This has been with me this whole time and has helped me build my own integrity.

### IV. Community & Impact

**Olga:**   How do you balance giving back to others while still taking care of yourself?

**Erika:** I think that giving back to others is also a way of self-care. When I see the world around me is positive, it is a reflection in myself. When I see a need for action, I will act, because when I know I have helped someone, I will feel good about myself and not live with the regret of what I could have done. I take care of myself so that the world around me is a good one, and I don't see a separation between the two elements.

**Olga:**  Have you found it easy or difficult to build friendships with Finnish women or from other countries ? Why?

**Erika:** I find it very easy to build friendships with people that are generally open minded and not judgmental. With women (and men) of around the world, I've noticed two distinct types:

Those who have never traveled or experienced the world. With this group, I find it challenging to connect due to our distant experiences of life, the fact that they find it uncomfortable to communicate in other languages, and what I perceive as cultural rigidity. The often see me as a temporary encounter.

Those who have traveled, lived abroad, speak multiple languages, and are more compassionate and flexible. With this group, I have managed to build very strong, deep, and honest relationships, which I consider the best friendships I've ever built.

**Olga:**   If you could create one new tradition for international women in Finland, what would it be?

**Erika:** I would create a day where women act as their alter ego: a day where we are allowed to enact the character we wish to be. I think this would be extremely powerful for manifesting and becoming one's highest self, especially since international women often need the courage to be.

V.  **Looking Ahead**

**Olga:**   What would your “ideal working life” in Finland look like 10 years from now?

**Erika:** I hope that 10 years from now, my ideal working life will be back to more hybrid and flexible working settings and will be more diverse. I expect the work-life balance to improve, with more part-time, contract, and freelance opportunities, and a move from five-day to four-day work weeks. I also hope, with the adoption of AI technologies, that our productivity increases, freeing up time to invest in self development, family, friends, and sports. My ideal working life would be much more flexible, open, agile, and easily adaptable to the different individual needs and diverse backgrounds and identities of all.

**Olga:**  Which problem faced by international women in Finland do you hope will be solved in the near future?

**Erika:** I think about the international women who come here (or anywhere in the world - as this is a global issue) to follow their spouse, especially those who come without mental preparation or access to resources, often ending up marginalized, isolated, or staying at home alone or with their children. I hope that in the future, this problem can be truly recognized and that institutions build a supporting system for these women to better integrate, contribute and be part of a social group.

**Olga:**    If you had the power to change one policy or cultural norm in Finland, what would it be?

**Erika:** If I could change one thing, it would be to encourage greater cultural flexibility in challenging the status quo. While the Finnish adherence to rules creates stability, a bit more openness to questioning norms could further fuel innovation and societal advancement. I also see an opportunity for more visible spontaneous generosity and community support to help both Finns and newcomers thrive.

**Olga:**  How do you personally define success — has that definition changed since moving here?

**Erika:** Success for me has always been tied to recognition, and that definition has not changed. It is primarily self-recognition and self-acceptance. I feel successful when I have done everything I set out to do, and I recognize it within myself; external recognition is just an added value.

**Olga:**  What new skill, dream, project or collaboration are you excited about pursuing next?

**Erika:** I am excited about pursuing an MBA in the near future with a focus on strategy and digitalization. I have been exploring educational programs globally and do not exclude the possibility of moving abroad to live in another country to learn, build a larger network, and grow professionally.
