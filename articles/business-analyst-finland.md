### I. Origins & Background — becoming a Business Analyst

**Olga K.:** Nataliia, please tell us a bit about your background and was there a specific “aha” moment when you realized you’re naturally the translator between business and tech, even before you had the title Business Analyst? What happened?

**Nataliia:** I believe the Business Analyst role requires developing skills beyond ”pure” IT: strong communication, the ability to understand business processes quickly, and to clearly document and explain them to diverse involved stakeholders. When I sought a way into software development, the BA path felt like the most natural fit: it leverages my strengths in uniting cross-functional teams, facilitating productive dialogue, and producing clear, actionable documentation.

**Olga K.:** If someone looked only at your early career path, what detour or “mistake” actually turned out to be your biggest advantage today?

**Nataliia:** My first master's degree is from the Pedagogical University. Although I never worked as a school teacher and for a long time regarded those years as a detour, I later recognized how wrong that assumption was. Studies in psychology, pedagogy, social sciences, mathematics, and physics helped shape my analytical mindset, taught me how to formulate incisive questions, locate reliable sources, and communicate clearly and persuasively with diverse audiences. What once seemed like a misstep has become a significant advantage in my career. Overall, every received education, every completed job, every mistake is just a piece of the puzzle of everyone's unique personality.
![Photo by Olga K.](/assets/Publications/Natali1.jpg "Photo by Olga K.")

**Olga K.:** What skill did you develop outside of work that surprisingly made you better at analysis or stakeholder communication?

**Nataliia:** I don’t consider myself a natural-born communicator or the best one in conducting communication sessions, but for sure, I’ve been fortunate to learn from skilled people around me. I recommend that everyone should cultivate and improve public-speaking skills, take any chance to work on them, because later they will pay dividends in stakeholder engagement and clarity of analysis. Improving this is a long-term commitment for me: I plan to take singing lessons to master breath control, vocal projection, and presence so I can speak with more confidence and impact, and hold the attention of my audience.

**Olga K.:** When you first started, what part of the BA role did you totally underestimate or misunderstand?

**Nataliia:** I didn't know how many meetings and document writing I am going to have in the future, how many interviews I am going to conduct, and how many questions to ask :)

**Olga K.:** If you had to explain your career journey as a product lifecycle (discovery → MVP → scale), what phase are you in now and why?

**Nataliia:** I'd call it Discovery Scaling: I am still onboarding myself in Finland, trying to increase my adaptiveness level. But it is a great feeling to start all over again in the new country. I have never known before how strong I am.

### II. The Idea & Innovation — specificity of being a BA in Finland

**Olga K.:** How does working in Finland change the way you run workshops or gather requirements compared to more hierarchical cultures?

**Nataliia:** It is worth mentioning that there is no “working in X country” anymore, at least in the IT field. Globalization is bringing people from different countries to your team or as customers. And the difference between cultures in the professional world is getting smaller and smaller. The way we work or communicate is unified nowadays. While globalization accelerates cross-border collaboration and standardization of processes, cultural nuances still influence how people respond to workshops, ask questions, and interpret requirements. The core practices above help bridge those nuances, ensuring effective discovery across diverse teams

I'd say it took some time for me to get used to the “light” way of reaching out to involved or “important” people. There is no need anymore to hesitate to ask the question or look for the correct words to make sure that I am conducting the discovery in the right way.
![Photo by Olga K.](/assets/Publications/Natali3.jpg "Photo by Olga K.")

**Olga K.:** Finnish work culture values trust and independence — how does that influence decision-making in projects you lead?

**Nataliia:** I am sure that trust and independence should be base for any relationship not only professional. The more people you lead, the more difficult it becomes to micro-manage everyone. The trust lets everyone focus on the big picture instead of controlling small details. And no one can combine different areas of expertise. You should rely on the professionalism of your colleagues and teammates.

In Finnish work culture, trust and independence guide decision-making. I empower teams to own their decisions by clearly defining objectives, decision rights, and success criteria, while maintaining transparency and accountability.

**Olga K.:** Have you ever had to “translate” not just between business and tech, but between different communication styles or cultural expectations? What did you learn?

**Nataliia:** Delivering negative feedback is the most challenging aspect of this type of translation for me. It often sparks the most controversy in international communication. It’s a skill I’m continually developing, not yet mastered. It’s an ongoing journey of learning and improvement.

**Olga K.:** What’s something uniquely Finnish about product development that outsiders might not immediately notice?

**Nataliia:** I’d call it “Nokia” product development style because almost everyone in my network who is older than 40 used to work at Nokia. It is impossible to bring some details or the samples. It’s rather an approach, the way to see the result at the beginning of the path. Maybe the main point is that there are no outsiders; it is easy to adapt, start working together, and achieve the expected results.

**Olga K.:** If you were designing the “ideal Finnish digital project from scratch,” what practices would you absolutely keep — and what would you redesign?

**Nataliia:** Don’t expect it to be ideal. Just do your best. Otherwise, there is a chance of failure in trying to reach the best results. Dreaming about the best could slow down the development. Or you can lose focus on the main idea, trying to polish the small features. And please do not try to achieve 100 percent of User satisfaction. We can't make everyone happy, but we can solve the main problems of business using digital tools.

### III. Relevance of Business & System Analysis to Finnish projects and business environment

**Olga K.:** What’s the most expensive misunderstanding you’ve seen that good analysis could have prevented?

**Nataliia:** The most expensive mistakes are taking place at the stage of the project idea. The wrong concepting long before the development started. The worst mistake is “This project should not be initiated”.

**Olga K.:** When time or budget is tight, how do you decide what level of analysis is “enough” without overengineering?

**Nataliia:** When the development team knows what to do and what result to reach. When all stakeholders have enough information to make decisions at their level.

**Olga K.:** Do you see yourself more as a risk-reducer or opportunity-creator — and why?

**Nataliia:** I am strong at reducing risk while actively seeking opportunities from every possible direction. I bring that same dual mindset to every engagement: safeguard delivery while continuously looking for ways to deliver more value.

But in my personal life, I am more about the risk.

**Olga K.:** What signals tell you early that a project is heading toward failure, even before metrics show it?

**Nataliia:** Poor communication rhythms like meetings being canceled, updates being inconsistent, or important info is siloed. There is a lot to mention.

**Olga K.:** If analysis disappeared from a project for a month, what subtle chaos would show up first?

**Nataliia:** I believe that the team can survive for one month based on existing backlog items. But after this, the size of the gap between the business needs and development could dangerously increase.

### IV. Tech, AI, Data & Ethics — reflections and real use in projects

**Olga K.:** Where do you personally draw the line between “smart automation” and “over-automation”?

**Nataliia:** I’d not call “automation” a complicated decision nowadays. There are a lot of well-defined frameworks for different processes. We just have to select the correct one wisely.

**Olga K.:** How do you balance data-driven decisions with human intuition or user empathy?

**Nataliia:** It is always reasonable to rely on measurable metrics. And if there is a chance to use numbers, I'd prefer to go with them. User empathy can easily bring the product into trouble. Human reaction is super difficult to predict, maybe even impossible for many cases.

**Olga K.:** If you had to design an AI product with zero historical data, how would you approach discovery and validation?

**Nataliia:** Actually, it could be risky to start, because to train the models and expect the correct output, the data should have high quality. But one of the possible ways could be finding at least related information from a similar field, and then iteratively update the data models as soon as data starts coming after the first release and piloting.

**Nataliia:** I definitely can’t “make the world a better place”, it is too abstract and not possible to achieve. But I can make the user experience better and easier, to offer an easier way to work and achieve the goals for Users for whom I am building my products.
