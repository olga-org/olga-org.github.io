Why Europe’s Next Innovation Powerhouse Might Be Led by This Bold Voice from Helsinki

Created on 2025-07-22 13:00

Published on 2025-07-23 06:57

Today, we have the pleasure of speaking with Roméo Rinne, a dynamic force in Finland’s innovation and entrepreneurship landscape. As Co-Founder of Venue Lab and Nexuses, and the Executive Director of Xes Helsinki, Roméo’s work sits at the intersection of technology, community building, and visionary leadership.

At Venue Lab, Roméo with the team is dedicated to empowering individuals and businesses to thrive within the dynamic world of live music. They state their mission as creation a more inclusive and vibrant ecosystem where grassroots musicians and local venues can connect, grow, and flourish. By bridging gaps in the live entertainment landscape, they aim to usher in a new era of accessible, sustainable, and community-driven musical experiences. Meanwhile, Nexuses is aiming to supercharge the Entrepreneurship Society (ES) ecosystem by providing the support needed to build world-class startup communities.

In his role at Xes Helsinki, Roméo leads a vibrant startup community that acts as a catalyst for early-stage founders, providing resources, peer support, and a launchpad for ideas that push boundaries. His ability to move fluidly between grassroots ecosystems and strategic foresight makes him a rare connector of people, ideas, and futures.

In this conversation, we explore Roméo’s perspective on building meaningful networks, scaling impact-driven ventures, and the future of founder support in Finland, Europe and beyond.

I. Origins & Founder’s and ED’s Background

Olga K.: Romeo, what’s a formative experience before Haaga Helia's entrepreneurship society (XES) that unexpectedly shaped your entrepreneurial DNA—something that “took root” only years later?

Roméo Rinne: Volunteering at Slush in 2019 as a group lead was definitely the start for my business and entrepreneurial career. After volunteering at Slush, I applied for university and the rest is history!

Olga: Outside tech and business, who’s been your most surprising mentor (an artist, philosopher, traveler…), and how did they influence your vision?

Roméo: As a big sports enthusiast, I take a lot of inspiration from athletes and their mentalities when it comes to having a stubborn and dedicated vision on things that you want to achieve. Lewis Hamilton for example often talks about the importance of adversity and the importance of overcoming things to grow.

Olga: In building XES, what part of your background felt like a creative rebellion—something you did against your upbringing or community norms?

Roméo: XES was my introduction into a similar role. For the start, I felt my background was not so in tune with the business world. However, this different viewpoint has been my greatest strength so far in my opinion. Doing things that are not the norm.

Olga: Was there an early moment when you felt like an impostor, and how did you process that to fuel your growth instead of stall it?

Roméo: Absolutely. Still to this day there are many moments I feel as an imposter. But what is important is to trust the process, keep working and realize that your self-doubt is rarely worth listening to.

Olga: What totally unrelated hobby or obsession do you still carry with you, and how does it occasionally sneak into your work mindset?

Roméo: Music and sports. I love to use the mindset from a sports background and the creativity from music to influence my work wherever possible.

II. The Idea & Innovation

Olga: Has a failed experiment within Venue‑Lab or Nexus unexpectedly revealed a new line of innovation you’re now pursuing?

Roméo: Definitely! Creative solutions from setbacks tend to be where new innovations are. It’s important to fail and to do it often.

Olga: How has a breakthrough in one XES strand (say Venue‑Lab) serendipitously influenced a completely different area, like Nexus or executive function?

Roméo: Yes and no, always with multiple projects simultaneously, ideally you find overlap that is mutually beneficial. However, this rarely happens unintentionally.

Olga: When was the last time you deliberately broke a “best practice” or conventional wisdom—and were glad you did?

Roméo: As any industry there are rules and guidelines that are there to be followed. However, an industry norm might not always be the best or fastest approach and people may admire your creativity. If the norm is an email, maybe try and give them a call instead!

Olga: How do you cultivate environments within XES where even the most serious thinkers feel safe to behave like children with big questions?

Roméo: Community building is difficult but it’s all about giving everyone a voice and creating a safe space where people can testbed and fail without judgment. I’m happy to see new people joining XES events with an open mind.

Olga: What’s a not-glorified but absolutely necessary part of innovation—something you wish people appreciated more?

Roméo: As mentioned, before I would say failure. Failure not only teaches you what not to do, but also creative solutions, thinking on your feet and is a great way to lose your ego.

III. Relevance of Finnish Startup Job Market, Talents & XES

Olga: Finland often breeds methodical problem-solvers. When have you actively flipped this script—harnessing Finnish restraint to spark spontaneous creativity?

Roméo: That almost feels like my job at this point! The world needs methodical problem-solvers just as it does creative ones. Having a diverse team that can problem-solve from multiple angles is so important. Finland tends to have more methodical problem-solvers, so my part tends to be more in the creative side here!

Olga:   How do you balance nurturing talent rooted in Finnish culture with preparing them to disrupt global markets immediately?

Roméo:   Staying true to the original idea as well as building a solution which is prepared for scale. Fast growth is great, but it is important to have a solid foundation first.

Olga: What roles or capabilities are still shockingly undervalued in the Finnish startup scene—and how is XES schooling founders to seek them out?

Roméo: In Finland specifically, proactivity in such a close community will stand out very fast. At XES we teach founders to be out there and to communicate clearly.

Olga:   Have you discovered any fringe skill sets (e.g., industrial designers, ethical hackers, systems thinkers) that became unexpectedly essential to your work?

Roméo: Definitely! We aim for a very non-hierarchical system at XES, and communication is a skill set we train a lot. Everybody should and is encouraged to tell and share ideas.

Olga: What talent have you seen incubated here in Helsinki that you’ve bet on exporting—and why do you think the world needs to see it?

Roméo: Right now, our project at NexUs, SPLASH! , and a future project we are working on is all about teaching European countries about Finland’s model for creating new founders. We just recently went to Athens with our new project. Together with our team and Peter Vesterbacka, we met with Team Panathēnea and Greek Universities to discuss and ideate what the future of Greek University entrepreneurship programs would look like.

IV. Tech, AI, Data & Ethics

Olga: Where have you intentionally embraced a tech trade-off—choosing speed over transparency or data without perfect consent—and how did you justify it?

Roméo: The only tech trade off we have embraced at Venue-Lab is building slower than we would have wanted to. We have a certain standard and those ethics we were not willing to trade off. I can’t say I regret it, we have a vision, and we are sticking to it! Building slower has allowed us to be extremely cost-effective with 100% ownership and a software that is continuously being upgraded

Olga: Where has Finnish tech/AI culture imparted blind spots or biases in your worldview, and how are you building counterbalances into your systems?

Roméo: Perhaps a general answer, but customer reviews. As a founder you want all your customers to benefit and enjoy using your service. Feedback is the number 1 way to improve, however it is still something as a founder that weighs on your mind.

Olga: How are you designing tech not just for scalability but for supporting vulnerable, fragile communities whose voices are often drowned in mainstream innovation?

Roméo: I think Venue-Lab is a perfect example for this. At Venue-Lab we help venues manage their finances and save time and money booking live musicians. But at the same time, our team is committed to supporting grass-root live musicians. We don’t charge a monthly fee or a commission from artists and we intend to keep it this way. Our niche is live musicians and by giving them a platform and a voice we believe we can give them opportunities not seen before.

V. Vision & Future

Olga: What is a major trend you hope to see reinstated—something lost or sidelined today that you believe needs to resurface by 2035?

Roméo: While it has never been a trend yet, I believe by 2035 it needs to be normalized for young people to lead innovation. Topics that may seem obvious to you and I, may be something completely unheard of for an older entrepreneur. True innovation needs to come from the up-coming generation. And the young people need the experienced entrepreneur’s support.

Olga: What’s a “crazy interruption” you secretly hope enters the XES journey—maybe contact with an extraterrestrial, or global mandate resetting tech norms?

Roméo: Europe becoming a powerhouse for innovation would certainly be a great “crazy interruption” for XES! More seriously, I hope that XES stays on the path that it is. Growing and scaling while itself being that crazy interruption in the industry.

Olga: If XES were to vanish tomorrow, what single ripple or cultural shift would you be most proud of having seeded in Helsinki or beyond?

Roméo: I hope XES is still here long after me, but I would be incredibly proud to say that no matter if you are a first or last semester student, we at XES have built a generation of students that is inspired and educated to go and try to build businesses that can make the world a better place than it was before.

Olga:   As XES scales, what “non-quantifiable” impact are you determined to protect—community, joy, serendipity?

Roméo: The community for sure. I’m not talking the size of the community either. I mean the people who attend our events and reach out to us for business help. We aren’t the biggest ES in Finland, but our community is something special for sure.

Olga: Ten years post‑XES, what will Roméo Rinne be doing—still in tech, teaching, writing, or bartering artisanal coffee on the side?

Roméo: That’s the question I’m working on! Firstly, I hope to scale my projects to their greatest potential and from there we will see. Could be in Finland or somewhere else. There is no future that I see where I’m not working on ideas and projects that I feel passionate about. Maybe all of the above, you never know!
