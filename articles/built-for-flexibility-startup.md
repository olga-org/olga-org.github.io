### II. The Idea & Innovation of Thermal Energy Storage Solutions

**Olga K.:** If you could explain your TES system in a metaphor from nature or art, what would it be and why?

**Emin:** I believe most close metaphor is blue berries. They capture unused renewable energy and convert it into another form of energy that we can use. Also, as we eat the fruits it continues to grow new berries. The only difference is our solution do not have seasons to work. It works all year long.

**Olga K.:** What is the most radical assumption you made in developing your technology — and how did you test whether it was true?

**Emin:** Reaching high heat storage temperatures and still using conventional heat transfer fluids build for lower temperatures to discharge is the radical change. We validated this using a bench-scale pilot system—roughly the size of a small room. This allowed us to prove that we could effectively 'discharge' the stored energy stably.

**Olga K.:** What’s a “myth” in thermal energy storage or heat technology that you’ve had to challenge in your work, and what was the reaction?

**Emin:** There are no myths but for some people do not accept the idea of turning electricity to heat and store that way very easily.

**Olga K.:** If you had unlimited research budget for one experiment that might fail but could revolutionize stored heat applications, what would you attempt?

**Emin:** Currently we are about to test it, but I will tell you after we test it. We find a rather lower cost way to test it.

**Olga K.:** How has the concept of efficiency in your innovations evolved — especially when balancing engineering, sustainability, and practical use-cases?

**Emin:** There is a trade of for scale of our system to the systems that it supports. As we increase our size, we can more easily apply more conventional solutions for larger sizes. Our trick lies on working on a define size optimized for predefined results.

### III. Relevance of Project & Finnish Needs


**Olga K.:** What unique characteristics of the Nordic climate or Finnish industry make thermal energy storage especially impactful here — and how would you explain that to someone from a country with not so radical changes in climate?

**Emin:** Calor-e capture electricity during the specific windows when it is most affordable—typically when our wind farms are at peak production—we transform that volatile power into a stable, high-temperature reservoir. This offers a quiet but profound sense of energy security, allowing a hospital or a food processing plant to draw from its own "thermal bank" during peak hours when the grid is strained and prices spike. It is a humble, intraday habit that makes decarbonization both sensible and seamless; we aren't trying to change the rhythm of the wind, we are simply being resourceful enough to hold onto its value so our industries can stay fossil-free and resilient, hour by hour.

**Olga K.:** In what ways have Finnish cultural values (e.g., sisu, resilience, sustainability) influenced the way you build and promote your solutions?

**Emin:** Our systems are made to endure a very wide operational range and atmospheric conditions. They are designed and manufactured with resilience in focus to operate with minimal effort at most demanding conditions. Also, all operation philosophy is based on co-existing with nature and benefit from its changes rather than fighting against it.

**Olga K.:** If Finland had zero incentives for clean tech but a severe heat crisis, how would your priorities or strategy change?

**Emin:** Not much because our system is mainly designed to solve a more challenging problem for helping electric grid to balance more supply changes due to renewables and more demand changes due to decarbonization of heat. These are evolutionary changes in world energy infrastructure and will continue to create demand.

**Olga K.:** What’s the most surprising industry or customer request you’ve received in Finland or Europe — and how did you respond?

**Emin:** We get a demand from Total Energy for one of their possible pilots with a relatively quite high capacity. We make a revised concept for their needs. Later they decide to test not a grid integrated but energy supply integrated solution. But that cooperation teaches us a lot.

**Olga K.:** How do you see your solutions interacting with other emerging Finnish energy innovations, like sand batteries or seasonal heat storage, in building a national clean-heat ecosystem?

**Emin:** While Finland needs large-scale seasonal storage to act as our national reserve, Calor-e provides a localized, high-output solution that works on a fast, intraday cycle. We capture green electricity during the specific windows when it’s most affordable, then discharge it as intense heat exactly when a factory or hospital needs it later that same day.

This distributed storage doesn’t compete with centralized systems; it complements them to create a more secure and resilient infrastructure. By having these smaller, supportive units spread out across the country, we ensure that our path to decarbonization isn't vulnerable to a single point of failure—like a physical disaster or a cyberattack. It’s a humble but vital way to keep our industries running on clean, secure energy, ensuring that if the main system is ever disrupted, the local warmth stays right where it’s needed.

### IV. Tech, AI, Data & Ethics Reflections


**Olga K.:** Do you see space for using AI in your project? Do you use AI for any routine tasks?

**Emin:** We do not directly use AI in our systems, but our systems are operable by AI based control systems to deliver maximum benefit for our customers. It can enhance demand forecasting, charging–discharging strategies, fault detection, and predictive maintenance. We already leverage AI-driven tools for routine tasks such as data analysis, performance monitoring, and operational optimization, allowing our engineering team to focus on higher-value innovation and system design. Other than that, we use AI in daily mundane tasks such as data clearance.

**Olga K.:** What is the most surprising insight you’ve gained from real-world data that challenged your original model or expectations?

**Emin:** Energy price fluctuations are increasing every day. This leads to nearly quarterly changes in our solutions operational models. Our system decision is entirely based on grid data for 10-to-60-minute data intervals. To solve this challenge, we are finalizing development of a digital twin to show potential results for customers based on real life data sets.

**Olga K.:** If your TES systems were to generate data that could predict future energy demand with high accuracy, what ethical questions would that raise?

**Emin:** Since our solutions are industrial capacity and used in mostly consumer products or their heating need, we do not expect any ethical problems. A higher accuracy will lead to lower waste in Finland energy generation capacity, and this will lead to lower prices and environmental impact.

**Olga K.:** How do you balance technological innovation with concerns about data privacy, sustainability, or unintended social impacts?

**Emin:** We approach innovation with a “responsible by design” mindset. This means embedding data privacy, cybersecurity, and lifecycle sustainability considerations directly into system architecture rather than treating them as afterthoughts. We prioritize data minimization, transparent data governance, and robust security practices, while continuously evaluating environmental and social impacts. Balancing progress with responsibility ensures our technologies deliver not only performance gains but also long-term trust and value for society.

**Olga K.:** Imagine a future where your system anticipates and responds autonomously to grid conditions — what human-in-the-loop decisions would you still insist on and why?

**Emin:** It’s about building a system that is as responsible as it is advanced; while our technology handles the complex task of providing affordable heat and driving decarbonization hour by hour, a human expert remains ready to step in during those rare, high-risk moments. For us, true energy security isn't just about smart software; it's about ensuring that the final layer of protection is always a thoughtful human eye, keeping our people and our infrastructure safe from any outside physical or cyber disruption.

### V. Vision & Future — Collaboration and Support


**Olga K.:** If Calor-e could collaborate with any institution — fictional or real (e.g., NASA, CERN, or Studio Ghibli) — who would it be and what would you build together?

**Emin:** Currently we are collaborating with several European research centers, but we plan to increase our technology capacity with new material search, AI based control algorithms and more robust manufacturing systems based on autonomous manufacturing lines for major parts of our products. We would collaborate with the VTT Technical Research Centre in Finland, along with KTH Royal Institute of Technology and the Fraunhofer Institute, to build a resilient, distributed thermal energy network. While larger systems handle the bulk of national energy, we would build localized units that offer high heat discharge for smaller industrial capacities, acting as a supportive layer for energy security. This distributed approach makes the grid much harder to disrupt through physical or cyber-attacks, ensuring that affordable heat and decarbonization stay on track even in a crisis.

**Olga K.:** How do you see the team and the product in a year? What you expect will be changed?

**Emin:** In a year, we expect clear growth in both our team and technology. The team will be larger, with added expertise in engineering, digitalization, and commercialization. Our product will be more refined, field proven, and easier to integrate into customer operations. We anticipate moving from early-stage scaling toward broader market adoption, stronger partnerships, and visible operational results.

**Olga K.:** What type of support — from policy makers, industry, or academia — would most accelerate your vision without compromising your core values?

**Emin:** The most valuable support for Calor-e is stable, long-term collaboration rather than just short-term incentives. We need clear and predictable regulatory frameworks from policymakers and early adoption or co-development partnerships from industry to help develop our manufacturing capacity. From academia, we look for joint research on materials, modelling, and optimization. This type of support helps us scale our technology effectively while preserving our core values of engineering integrity, sustainability, and practical impact.

**Olga K.:** How would you design an ideal multi-stakeholder pilot project to demonstrate your technology at a national level — and what unexpected partner would you invite?

**Emin:** Our ideal pilot project partners are Finnish energy companies, particularly those municipal utilities that manage both the electrical grid and district heating networks. Because of this unique integrated structure, our technology can serve as a bridge between the two: we provide critical flexibility services by drawing power when it is most affordable and abundant, then converting it into a steady supply of heat for their customers. This allows these companies to balance the grid more effectively while simultaneously decarbonizing their heating systems. By focusing on these co-development partnerships, we can refine our manufacturing capacity alongside the very experts who manage our national energy infrastructure. This collaborative approach ensures that our distributed storage serves as a practical, high-impact tool for energy security, keeping the system resilient and the heat accessible through every intraday shift.

**Olga K.:** Thinking 10 years ahead, what is one goal that feels “ambitious and scary,” and why is it still worth pursuing?

**Emin:** Our ten-year goal is to build a robust manufacturing network across Europe to meet the growing demand for high-temperature industrial heat. We aim to replace a significant number of fossil fuel burners, focusing specifically on the heavy industrial processes that operate at temperatures beyond the capabilities of standard heat pumps. By scaling our production through these regional hubs, we can provide an affordable and practical path to decarbonization for industries that currently have limited green alternatives. This expansion will enhance energy security by creating a localized supply chain of thermal storage units, allowing factories to transition to clean, stored energy while maintaining the high intensity heat their production requires.

**Olga K.:** If you could write an open letter to future collaborators or funders, what would be the single most important message you’d want them to understand about your mission?

**Emin:** Our goal is to make the electrification of heat a practical and affordable reality for both industry and society.
