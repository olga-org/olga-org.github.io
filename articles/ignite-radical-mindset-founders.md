# “If It Frustrates You, You’re on the Right Path”: The Radical Mindset Behind Ignite’s Next Generation of Founders

Today we are speaking with [Annika Blomqvist](https://fi.linkedin.com/in/annika-linnea-blomqvist) , Co-Director of [Ignite](https://fi.linkedin.com/company/ignite-by-aaltoes) , the 10-week founder launchpad run by the student-led Aalto Entrepreneurship Society, and [Kristaps Grieznis](https://fi.linkedin.com/in/kgrieznis) , Co-Director of Ignite as well as a driving force behind redefining how early-stage founders build, launch, and scale their ventures. Ignite is a dynamic accelerator based in Espoo that turns young ambition into real founder action, providing mentorship, community and hands-on support to the next generation of founders. Together, Annika and Kristaps are shaping an evolving pathway for student-driven entrepreneurship and innovation in Finland and beyond.

### I. Origins & Background

**Olga:** Annika and Kristaps, please tell us a bit about your background and experience.

**Kristaps:** (With a huge smile) I originally grew up in the Latvian countryside. Being the oldest of seven kids, I learned to manage chaos pretty early on. Entrepreneurial path kicked in around 11 when I started finding ways to earn my own money, and it eventually pulled me to Riga at 16 to study advertising.
A big turning point was landing an internship at OSMO, a design studio in Italy. That was a masterclass in juggling diverse projects and really understanding what means work with clients. I took all the learnings back to Latvia, set up my own consulting practice, and started building brands for local cafes and hosting techno events. It was a great sandbox and profitable, but I realized I wanted to operate on a bigger scale. So, at age of 23 I relocated to Helsinki to study Design at Aalto. Since then, it has been a snowball effect into the Nordic ecosystem, building out projects with [Aalto Entrepreneurship Society](https://fi.linkedin.com/company/aalto-entrepreneurship-society), Vercel, OP Bank, [Slush](https://fi.linkedin.com/company/slush), and connecting design with the startups! Currently I lead an startup accelerator Ignite, and I am more excited for what's coming next than ever!
![Photo by Olga K.](/assets/Publications/KristapsAnnika1.jpg "Photo by Olga K.")

**Annika:** I have always been interested in entrepreneurship, since I’ve perceived it as a way to bring your own vision to life. I’ve followed my parents’ entrepreneurship journeys closely, and I got excited about the business and scaling part of it. I’m currently in my 3rd year of bachelor’s in [Aalto University School of Business](https://fi.linkedin.com/company/aalto-university-school-of-business), majoring in Management. I found [Ignite Aaltoes](https://fi.linkedin.com/in/ignite-aaltoes-7342702aa) by applying to Ignite myself, and although I didn’t make it to the batch in the end, I joined the Demo Day organizing team and started doing all kinds of operations in Aaltoes. It was a true rabbit hole in the autumn of 2025. It was super inspiring to finally find a community, which shares the same ambition and willingness to put in the work. Work-wise, I have worked a lot in different small businesses, and most recently, I was in the development team of a startup validation course in Aalto University.

**Olga:** What’s one seemingly unrelated experience from your past (e.g., a hobby, travel, job) that surprisingly influences how you lead an accelerator like Ignite today?

**Kristaps:** If something frustrates you, its a sign that you have chosen the right path. Few years ago I hitchhiked across Portugal. It was a hot summer day and I ran out of water, my phone battery was dead and I was in the middle of nowhere close to the highway. I managed to stop a car that went the other direction and negotiated that he drives me to closest safe spot. We quickly became friends and he ended up driving me 2h to the other direction and I ended up in the most beautiful Portuguese beach, Aveiro.
Since then, whenever I get stuck in my work day, I remind myself that difficult situations always come to end.

**Annika:** A few years ago I travelled to Morocco with my friend. We ended up at a wrong harbor in the middle of the night, without the local currency and with no other tourists around since it was a cargo ferry. We had to improvise and managed to get a local man to negotiate a taxi for all of us. We missed our night train and had to find accommodation on the go. We managed to find a hotel with 24/7 reception after driving 40 minutes to the right place.
Even though the situation could’ve ended badly, I realized that you have to be able to adapt to the situation and make quick decisions. Staying agile when your plan doesn’t go how you anticipated has helped already during these few months of running the accelerator and is extremely important as a founder as well.

**Olga:** If so far were a startup, what would its pitch deck look like, from the problem you solved to key pivot moments?

**Kristaps:** What a great question!
If I were to make a pitch deck, the title would be “24h Life Rebrand Sprint”. It would be a very personal story, but totally worth reflecting on. Last October, within one week, I quit my job, long-term relationships, left volunteering team at Slush and moved to a new country. I decided to open a new, fresh page and sketched out three possible options for my life. The pitch-deck would have strong emphasis on the wellbeing tools used, such as fixing sleep, developing a suitable routine, cutting out unhealthy pleasures that deliver instant dopamine, reflection via writing and applying strategic thinking directly to personal life / career matters.

**Annika:** To be honest, my career pitch deck would be full of pivots. The longest I have stayed in one position is around 1,5 years. I have always taken action quickly and without doubt, if I seized the opportunity. For example, I got my photographer assistant job by randomly DMing someone I saw on Instagram, and she ended up becoming my friend and we had amazing adventures together.

**Olga:** What’s a question you used to dread being asked about your background, and how would you answer it honestly now?

**Kristaps:** When I was younger, I was completely triggered when somebody asked about my age. I looked much older and wanted to be older. Now I'm 26 and I wish I could be 16 again.
Julius Thimm, Ex-CPO of Slush, once asked me about to tell about personally difficult situation in work-life and how I solved it. Back then that question was uncomfortable.
Now, I would bring up the Ignite launch day back in February 2nd, when we hosted 200 guests. I had a terrible idea to pre-schedule our new website launch during our live event, the “apply” button did not work and there were some weird UI glitches too. What a mistake!

**Annika:** Naturally, many have asked how I ended up leading Ignite. The truth is that I don’t have a startup background, and I gained most of the knowledge during the fall of 2025 when I truly fell into the ES rabbit hole. I used to play myself a bit down for that, but now I think that the journey is a good example on how fast things may happen in this environment and how this whole ecosystem really is a human accelerator.

### II. The Idea & Innovation of Ignite accelerator
**Olga:** There are many accelerator programs in Finland, what makes Ignite different from others?

**Kristaps:** Ignite is a place for those who are not afraid to dream. We’re highly selective and take only the best, providing practically everything you need to excel as a founder. Cash for food, living place, 24/7 hardware labs. Our teams are coached by strong executives and niche-specific professionals, and we’re backed up by growth and education focused foundations and organizations that operate across hardware and software startup sectors. If you have a crazy idea, we want to hear about it.

**Annika:** Ignite is unique in many ways. It has a long history under Aaltoes, although the summer program has had different names and forms throughout the years. However, the main value of it is the advantage it has regarding the location in the central startup hub in Otaniemi campus, near Keilaniemi and Helsinki. It has established its reputation amongst the ecosystem attracting a lot of attention also from abroad, and is being considered as a serious alternative for a regular summer job.

**Olga:** What’s a belief about startup acceleration that Ignite intentionally challenges or rewrites?

**Annika:** Fundamentally, Ignite is not a startup accelerator, but a human accelerator. We are not hunting unicorns, even though some of the companies built in Ignite are inevitably growing and scaling fast. We believe in long-term impact through the people and their mindset. It may not be exactly this startup that succeeds, but eventually, with the network, skills, and courage that they gain over the summer they will build something big in the future.

**Kristaps:** We’re rewriting the stigma about entrepreneurship and startups. Even though entrepreneurship is not for all, it is overlooked by many who could do great things. People often think that successful people are money driven, which may be partly the case, but many successful people are driven by challenges and innovation for better society good.

**Olga:** What’s the most unorthodox metric you use (or wish you could use) to judge Ignite’s success that goes beyond funding or customer acquisition?

**Annika:** Ignite being often the first touchpoint to entrepreneurship, we can also measure the program itself by the people who realize that entrepreneurship as a career path is not for them. This is completely fine, since it is effectively made to be a safe environment to test building a business in a risk-free environment. However, this means that the program can’t romanticize the founder's journey, but also glorify the toxic hustle culture that may lead to burnout.

**Olga:** How does Ignite fuel creative intuition in founders, especially when “data-driven decisions” push the opposite direction?

**Annika:** We talk a lot about “founder’s hunch”, which means the gut feeling about a situation regarding the business. I personally believe that data, when collected with suitable methods measuring the right things, gives usually very good perspective and direction. However, this has to be paired with human intuition and observation. Especially when it comes to team dynamics, partnerships, customers, and other multi-factor decisions, it is important to listen to your gut. For example, a partnership may look great on paper, but your intuition may catch some vibes that are telling the opposite from cues that your conscious mind can’t even point out. Founder’s hunch may also apply to sensing a potential market which has not even yet been proved by market research.

**Kristaps:** Creativity can be achieved by providing safe space for experiments, which is exactly what we do. We also understand that taking a break is important, which we do by organizing outdoor activities, playing tennis, cooking together, going on hikes and more.

**Olga:** If Ignite could work with a startup outside of tech, say a cultural initiative or art collective, what would that partnership look like?

**Kristaps:** We support people no matter if it is tech, cultural NGO's or education sector. My mother and grandmother both were teachers, so the importance of education runs in my veins. If I have to choose one now, I would say to take initiative and organize an event inspired by “Live Aid” in 1985. It was a fund rising initiative that raised more than $100 millions, made big rockstars participate for free and provided an unforgettable moment for all across the globe.

### III. Ignite and Emerging Finnish Tech & Innovation Ecosystem

**Olga:** Finland’s ecosystem has iconic events like Slush, Arctic 15, Junction and others, what gap do you still see for early-stage founders before they reach global stages like that?

**Annika:** The gap is closing. The question is: how can we spread the awareness of these events and the startup ecosystem especially in Otaniemi and other ES’s in Finland to those who don’t yet know about the possibilities they can offer. The crucial step is to get involved in these ecosystems at least in some way, and the rest is usually history.

**Olga:** In your view, what unique strength does the Finnish startup ecosystem have that the rest of the world misunderstands or underestimates?

**Kristaps:** Hardware. Hardware. Hardware. Additionally, no competition, easy networking and quick reach. If you go to San Francisco, the market is oversaturated. Here, in Finland, we can name few big organizations, some venture capitals and only few unicorns. We have a huge playground that we can use to grow rapidly. Also, our education system is incredibly smartly connected together with startup ecosystem, and both of them work together toward overlapping goals.

**Annika:** The Finnish startup ecosystem is unique because a huge part of it is run by students and young people in general. They start initiatives and found organizations that scale without external push, and are willing to be all in because of the mission itself. This way of operating may have something to do with “sisu” — if there is no such thing yet, you’ll do it yourself even if people doubt it. Also the alumni community is a crucial part of the whole ecosystem: the default of all activity in the ecosystem is pro bono.

**Olga:** Which global innovation ecosystem do you think Finland could learn from and why?

**Annika:** San Francisco and Silicon Valley. And luckily there is a good information flow already between these two ecosystems, but the speed and grand thinking is something we could definitely adapt more of.

**Olga:** How can student-led communities like Aaltoes shape policy decisions or national strategies, not just support founders?

**Kristaps:** They absolutely can and must.
Here is an example - recently a petition EU-INC was developed by young European entrepreneurs. The petition suggests to remove the national barriers between European countries, developing an overlapping goals where you could open a company in any country digitally within 48 hours. The petition reached European Union, was accepted and currently is in the implementation stage. For me, this is incredibly inspiring and I cannot wait to see where it goes. Morale - if you have an idea, no matter how big or small, gather likeminded people and it can become truth on a global level.

**Annika:** Politicians and other stakeholders closely follow what’s happening in these circles. Especially recently startups have been stated as the possible saviour of Finland economic-wise. I strongly believe that by getting more young people involved in these initiatives we can see an effect already in relatively short time.

**Olga:** What unexpected industries (e.g., climate tech, gaming, education, social systems) are you most excited about emerging from Finland next?

**Kristaps:** Optic DeepTech. Companies like IXI eyewear, built 10 minutes from Aalto University campus, are very close to release their auto-focus glasses to the consumer market. I recently visited their laboratory and got to try the glasses out myself, I was simply in awe. Anything related to hardware here in Finland has a really high potential to grow, because many great engineers have stayed here since Nokia times and the the hardware education is passed to the next generations.

**Annika:** I’m keen to see emerging software in hardware solutions from Finland. We also have a lot of deep tech know-how and a large innovation base that lays a solid foundation for innovation.
### IV. Tech, AI, Data & Ethics, Reflections on How Startups Should Use Them

**Olga:** AI become frequently used instrument in our days. What’s one ethical dilemma around AI that you think most founders should take into consideration as early as possible and to find the answer for themself?

**Kristaps:** Independence on AI tools is a really serious problem. I believe in right and wrong ways to use them. So called “Godfather of AI” Geoffrey Hinton emphasizes, that it is really important how we interact with it. Blindly giving tasks is not healthy, similarly like scrolling Instagram Reels. Our brain is not developed to consume such unfiltered and misleading information on such big scale.

**Olga:** How should early-stage founders balance experimental tech (e.g., generative AI) with the responsibility to protect people and society?

**Kristaps:** Taking part in creating misleading reality is something that can backfire easily. I use AI tools to enhance learning process in work, yet I believe there.

**Olga:** Looking at today’s AI hype, what’s an overvalued or undervalued trend that you think will reshape startups in the next few years?

**Kristaps:** Ability to learn faster and integrating AI tools to improve individual skills.

### V. Vision & Future of Ignite and Collaborations

**Olga:** So, let's summarize, [Ignite](https://fi.linkedin.com/company/ignite-by-aaltoes) is more than a 10-week program: it is an entry point into a mindset. Built by the [Aalto Entrepreneurship Society](https://fi.linkedin.com/company/aalto-entrepreneurship-society) , Ignite operates as an equity-free founder launchpad in Espoo, supporting early-stage teams with mentorship, grants, housing, and a deeply engaged peer community. What stands out is not just the access to resources, but the culture: founders are encouraged to experiment boldly, fail safely, and grow fast — not only as builders, but as individuals navigating uncertainty for the first time.
At the same time, Ignite is quietly redefining what an accelerator can be. Rather than focusing purely on metrics like funding or rapid scaling, it emphasizes long-term impact through people — their mindset, resilience, and ability to collaborate. The program’s close ties to Finland’s startup ecosystem, combined with its openness to international founders and cross-industry ideas, position it as a natural bridge between student ambition and real-world innovation. It is as much a community platform as it is a launchpad.
Looking ahead, Ignite’s future lies in thoughtful expansion through collaborations, with startups, institutions, creatives, and emerging technologies alike. Its direction suggests something bigger than growth for its own sake: a commitment to shaping a generation that builds with intention, curiosity, and responsibility. In that sense, Ignite is not just preparing founders for the future, it is actively helping design what that future could look like.
And now comes the most exciting part: watching it all unfold in real time. Let’s keep an eye on how the next Ignite summer cohort takes shape: which ideas gain momentum, how teams come together, and what kinds of startups emerge from this intense, transformative experience. Chances are, some of them will soon become the stories everyone is talking about, the next signals of where the Finnish and global startup ecosystem is heading.
