I. Origins & Founder’s Background

Olga: Yuri, your journey blends business and IT—was there a moment when tech obsession collided head‑on with a hard business reality?  

Yuri Zubarovskiy: This is a classic point of tension between business and technology: engineers want to deliver as many features as possible, but that often clashes with time, resource, and risk constraints. We had a project to modernize a logistics system for a large client, and technically there was enormous potential for improvement. We enthusiastically started work right after signing the contract. But it quickly became clear that the resources for testing and stabilizing the changes simply couldn’t support the scale we envisioned. We had to move in very small iterations, constantly balancing ambition with stability. That experience taught me that even the most powerful ideas need to be implemented gradually—with ongoing stabilization—otherwise technical debt spirals out of control.

Olga: Before SuperFolders, did you ever envision AI-agents becoming your core offering, or was it a pivot born from personal pain points?

Yuri: Before SuperFolders, my Finnish friend and I launched a small project to help Airbnb apartment owners automate communication with guests—especially at night and on weekends. That’s when the concept of AI agents first emerged: not just responding to messages but coordinating actions with people like cleaners and maintenance crews. I was responsible for the technical part and built the core technology enabling agents to interact with multiple parties in real-time.  The next step was building the core of SuperFolders—a platform designed to simplify business process automation using AI agents and make it accessible to non-technical users. Our goal is to empower anyone to design efficient workflows by simply explaining their goals to the system—no code, no engineering team required.

Olga: What unconventional hobby or side project in your early career unexpectedly shaped SuperFolders’ DNA?

Yuri: Since childhood, I’ve had a habit of taking things apart—especially complex devices—to understand how they work. That curiosity led me to explore computer hardware, Linux systems, and eventually to prototyping my own gadgets during university. Later, I developed logistics automation solutions. In recent years, I’ve been actively exploring and testing AI technologies. SuperFolders is essentially the culmination of all that experience—aimed at making robotics usable in business without requiring deep technical expertise.

Olga: How would your younger, purely technical self react to seeing SuperFolders not just as tech, but as a human workspace?

Yuri: My younger, purely technical self would’ve been thrilled to see that the technology didn’t end up as just an idea or prototype gathering dust—but actually made its way into people’s hands, helping them solve real, everyday challenges.

Olga: Who’s the one mentor—outside your direct field—whose advice still echoes in your strategic decisions?  

Yuri: One of my earliest mentors was a professor of instrumentation engineering who always said: “You eat the elephant one bite at a time.” That simple phrase, combined with my love of complex systems, shaped my belief that any task can be broken down into manageable steps and completed.  Another major influence was Alexander, under whose leadership we built a digital bank. He used to say: “It should be hard for engineers, so it can be easy for users.” I’ve carried that principle into SuperFolders—I want our engineering team to always face serious challenges so we can create powerful, simple tools for the end users.

II. The Idea & Innovation

Olga: SuperFolders offers hands-on robotics kits to reduce pilot friction—was that inspired by a personal ‘failed pilot’ experience?  

Yuri: It all started when I bought a robot dog out of curiosity—just to see what it could do. Despite my broad technical experience, I quickly realized how difficult it was to make it perform complex, multi-step tasks—especially for non-engineers. It wasn’t the robot’s fault, but rather the lack of intuitive ways to integrate it into real-world operations. Still, you immediately sense its potential: if used correctly, such robots could handle a wide range of business tasks. That was the moment I realized the need for SuperFolders Robotics kits—practical tools that lower the barrier to entry and help teams move from experimentation to real-world value.

Olga: What was the most surprising real‑world use case you didn’t expect your robot dog kit to handle?

Yuri: A surprising and very welcome discovery was how confidently the robot dog navigates the forest. We hadn’t even started building a dedicated forest-mobility skill yet—taking into account terrain, fallen trees, stumps, grass, and branches—but it already showed strong baseline capabilities. That gave us a major push to develop its functionality specifically for unpredictable natural environments.

III. Relevance of Finnish Startup Market and Your Project

Olga: With SuperFolders headquartered in Finland, how do ‘sisu’ and high trust show up in your team’s ethos?  

Yuri: In the spirit of sisu, our SuperFolders Robotics system helps humans and robots work together—overcoming technical failures, organizational friction, and even harsh weather conditions. We believe resilience, trust, and clear coordination enable both teams and autonomous robot dogs to succeed where others stop.

Olga: What surprising advantage do Finnish engineers bring to implementing high‑precision robotics?

Yuri: Finnish engineers are incredibly strong in building reliable and fault-tolerant industrial systems.

Olga: Your pilot in Joensuu with LUKE hints at local collaboration—what made Finnish forestry uniquely compelling for robotics?  

Yuri: In forest management, many critical tasks need to be done on the ground—not from the air (as is common with drones today). This includes forest inventory, seedling monitoring and planting, thinning, and tree treatment. We believe that quadruped robots can potentially take on some of these tasks. They are autonomous, physically durable, capable of traversing rough terrain, and can perform operations where human presence has traditionally been required.

Olga: If you were to export a Finnish‑style talent model via SuperFolders, what cultural trait would be its cornerstone?  

Yuri: I’ve always felt connected to forestry—it’s a major part of Finnish culture and economy. I believe mobile robotics can integrate into this sector in a way that enhances human roles rather than replacing them. The image of a Finnish forester working with robotic assistance in challenging natural environments could become a powerful symbol of true human–robot symbiosis—where trust, resilience, and collaboration are more than just values; they’re the foundation of how we work together.

IV. Tech, AI, Data & Ethics

Olga: Robotics pilots sound exciting—but what’s your protocol when a robot dog goes rogue mid-demo?  

Yuri: At the pilot stage, all robot actions are manually controlled—we’re always nearby and ready to intervene if anything goes wrong. The main goal now is to collect as much behavioral data as possible in real-world conditions to better predict and prevent failures. We’re working to minimize the need for user intervention by gradually increasing monitoring points and building out recovery scenarios. For example, during one of our forest tests, we identified repeat cases where the robot got stuck while trying to climb over fallen trees or became entangled in branches. These moments showed us the need for specialized motion routines that help the robot autonomously handle such obstacles.

V. Vision & Future

Olga: Which stalled dream (AI or robotics feature) are you saving for a sequel—or never?  

Yuri: I’ve long envisioned a business automation system capable of orchestrating and conducting complex, fragmented processes like a symphony—where company goals and priorities serve as the musical score. It’s more than just an AI assistant—it’s an operating system for future organizations. Now, that vision is expanding to include mobile helper robots, opening the door to automating not only the digital realm but also the physical world. Robots that can move, act, interact with objects, and extend the reach of automation far beyond the screen.
