# [Why I Mentor: Beyond Knowledge Transfer to Mutual Transformation](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/why-i-mentor-beyond-knowledge-transfer-mutual-olga-kryuchkova-ocnjf)

*Published on 2025-06-10 17:23*

When I first joined the LUT mentoring programme as a mentor in 2023/2024, I assumed my role would be all about giving—sharing insights, offering guidance, helping someone navigate the early stages of their career. What I didn’t anticipate was just how much I’d gain in return.

Through the programme, I had the pleasure of meeting Lisa Tereshchenko —an outstanding researcher and future Big Four consultant, currently working as a business analyst while wrapping up her PhD at LUT University . What started as a mentorship turned into a true partnership of knowledge exchange—and, as a bonus, a great friendship. Who knew mentoring could double as professional development *and* networking with a side of laughter?

Fast forward to the 2024/2025 season, and I hit the intellectual jackpot again . This time, I got to work with Andrei Zhuravlev, who's deep in the fascinating world of direct force control for active magnetic bearings. (Yes, it sounds intense—and it absolutely is.) Andrei is now finalizing his PhD on this high-demand engineering solution. It’s been a privilege to contribute where I can and learn plenty along the way.

![Pictures by Ahti Brummer](/assets/Publications/mentoring251.jpg)

From our very first conversation with my mentees, it became clear: mentoring isn’t just about passing on knowledge. It’s about creating a space for curiosity, challenge, and mutual growth. I didn’t just answer questions—I was asked new ones. I didn’t just guide—I recalibrated, helped to find new angles of vision. My mentee’s fresh perspective pushed me to rethink assumptions, revisit ideas, and reflect on my own professional journey through a whole new lens.

![Pictures by Ahti Brummer](/assets/Publications/mentoring252.jpg)

The final dinner provided a particularly meaningful opportunity to connect with fellow mentors and mentees. One memorable encounter was with [Jenny Liimatta](https://www.linkedin.com/in/jenny-liimatta?miniProfileUrn=urn%3Ali%3Afs_miniProfile%3AACoAADiQMpkBFkSQvWFOfLJlBZEM3lu4cAVDM4s) , a Master's student in Economics and Business Administration, and her mentor [Maria Vickholm](https://www.linkedin.com/in/mariavickholm?miniProfileUrn=urn%3Ali%3Afs_miniProfile%3AACoAAAGIlloBD9Yyl1iunW3c68YU-1X5zNSUaq8) , a senior lecturer at Haaga-Helia specializing in Digital Marketing and Marketing Communications. Together, we exchanged insights and reflections on our mentoring experiences, deepening our understanding of the diverse paths within the program. It was a fitting and inspiring conclusion—one that underscored the shared sense of purpose, learning, and community that had grown throughout the journey.

![Pictures by Ahti Brummer](/assets/Publications/mentoring253.jpg)

I’d heard the horror stories—mentoring can be difficult, awkward, and in some cases, even counterproductive. But I quickly realized that such reputations usually stem from one thing: lack of preparation and a vague, “figure it out as you go” approach.

Thankfully, the LUT mentoring programme is the complete opposite. The structure is clear, the goals are defined, and the support is there not just for mentees, but for mentors too (yes, we like guidance as well! :). It’s a rare example of a mentoring setup where everyone knows what they’re doing—and if not, Anni Hänninen does. Big thanks for the thoughtful organization and continued support, Anna. With mentoring this smooth, I there is a high probability for me sign up again next year—for the knowledge, new exploits *and* the coffee chats.

I'd like to finalize this post with a short reflective analysis of the strengths and challenges inherent in mentoring programs based on my experience as a mentor and in the role of mentee too (Luckan mentoring program "Job buddies", successfully supervised by Julia Qesteri ), with particular emphasis on strategies for minimizing potential drawbacks and enhancing overall impact.

![Positive and challenging aspects of mentoring](/assets/Publications/mentoring255.png)

Challenge/ solution, strengthening strategy

1\. Misaligned Expectations

From the outset, we established shared goals as early in the process as possible to ensure alignment and clarity. We formalized our collaboration through written mentoring agreements and used the initial kickoff meetings to define what success would look like. In each subsequent meeting, we revisited expectations, allowing them to evolve flexibly in response to changing circumstances and emerging needs.

2\. Time Constraints

We initially agreed on a realistic meeting schedule—one hour per month—to ensure consistency without overcommitment. However, we often found ourselves connecting more frequently, as meaningful insights and topics emerged on a near-weekly basis. Between meetings, we utilized asynchronous tools such as email and shared Word documents to track progress and keep the momentum going, ensuring the mentoring process remained active and continuous.

3\. Lack of Structure

We made use of the recommended mentoring journal and checklist to bring structure and clarity to the process. LUT’s approach—emphasizing thematic sessions and practical “homework” assignments—helped guide our discussions and ensure consistent progress. Even a shared Word document proved valuable in maintaining focus and organization. Additionally, Anni played a key role in keeping us on track and well-prepared ahead of deadlines, ensuring that the mentoring experience remained both purposeful and productive.

4\. Communication Gaps

We established clear ground rules from the beginning, including meeting frequency, preferred communication channels, and the tone of our interactions. I also encouraged open dialogue around cultural and personality differences, recognizing that effective mentoring is built on transparency, mutual respect, and a genuine openness to diverse perspectives and experiences.

5\. Limited Outcomes

For us, the mentoring process resembled a series of agile project sprints—each cycle included built-in mini-reviews, such as midway check-ins and end-of-cycle reflections, to ensure we remained aligned and on track. These regular moments of assessment allowed us to adapt as needed and maintain steady progress. We also made it a point to celebrate “small wins” along the way—whether it was polishing a CV or receiving a new interview invitation—recognizing that these milestones are essential markers of growth and momentum.
