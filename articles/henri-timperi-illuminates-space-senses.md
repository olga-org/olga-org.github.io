# In Search of the Human: Henri Timperi Illuminates Space, Senses, and Self

Designer and creative entrepreneur [Henri Timperi](https://www.henritimperi.com/) brings together craftsmanship, material sensitivity, and immersive spatial thinking in his multidisciplinary practice. With a background rooted in furniture making and fine arts, his work explores the emotional and sensory potential of objects—particularly through light, sound, and material interaction.
In this conversation, Henri reflects on the origins of his practice, the intuitive process behind his [Midnight Sun Glass Light](https://www.henritimperi.com), and his vision for future design at the intersection of nature, technology, and human wellbeing. Drawing inspiration from Northern landscapes and seasonal phenomena, his work translates fleeting natural experiences into tangible, atmospheric design objects that invite calm, presence, and connection.
![Photo by Lauri Heikkinen](/assets/Publications/Henri1.jpg "Photo by Lauri Heikkinen")

### I. Origins & Background
**Olga:** Henri, can you please tell us a bit about your background and how your current project started?

**Henri:** I'm a designer, artist and creative entrepreneur. My background is in furniture design and manufacturing before my Master of Arts studies at Aalto School of Arts and Design, as I also have education as a cabinet maker and wood craftsman. I have long history working with visual arts, which combines well in my creative practice with design.
I'm very material and craft oriented when I design objects. Deep understanding of different materials and their qualities are very important to me. With that I can show the beauty of them in my works. I most often work with wood, glass, ceramics and metals like bronze or brass in my designs. Working with blown glass is different than other materials, because it’s always teamwork, with one or several glassblowers and assistants. There must be planned choreography among the team to get the best results and I really enjoy it.
My design career has evolved in lighting and audio design, because immersive spatial design interests me. I've been working with glass design and craft especially in sculptural lighting context for few years now. The Midnight Sun Glass Light is one of my latest creations in this field.

**Olga:** What’s the first object you remember making — and how does that piece still influence your practice today?

**Henri:** First real object I designed and made was a chair in the beginning of my cabinet maker education almost fifteen years ago. It taught me a lot about making a finished ergonomic product with the goal in mind that it would last for decades when manufactured properly. The process of making teaches patience and resilience. If parts of the product get damaged during the manufacturing phase, you start over from the beginning, as many times needed for creating a well-made design object. I still have the chair.

### II. The Idea & Innovation of the Project
**Olga:** What was the first sensation, image, or question that set this current (lamp) project in motion — a sound, landscape, memory, or something else?

**Henri:** I've spent a lot of time in Northern Finland in my life, and the nature there is very beautiful and close to my heart. I go there every year in the winter and summer. In summertime the midnight sun is a magical phenomenon, and I hike up the fells at midsummer and admire the sun over the beautiful vast view. I wanted to share this experience with others, so I translated this light phenomenon into a design lighting object, which creates warm atmospheric lighting to home environment and reminds us about our connection to nature. The inspiration for this Midnight Sun glass light comes from the beautiful nature of the North, and the never setting sun that colors the sky golden and orange.
The Midnight Sun glass light is about warm summer atmosphere and the unique light reflections it creates to the surrounding space. The glass surface and light source together create the reflection pattern that resembles golden midnight sun over a rippling lake.
I want to bring connection with nature and its beauty with calm and warm atmosphere to homes and shared spaces where people can enjoy and relax. Later I want to expand the Midnight Sun concept into a lighting collection.
The main idea behind the light source is that it creates this wavy effect, and all the other electrical components than the LED filaments are hidden outside the glass object, which makes it more slender and minimalistic compared to standard light bulbs. I can do endless variations of lighting fixtures with this combination.

**Olga:** Which innovation in this work came not from tech or tools, but from an intuition?

**Henri:** The light reflection patterns, which I have designed beyond this Midnight Sun project, emerged from intuition and mental visualization. After forming a clear image in my mind, I moved on to sketching, followed by creating molds and developing prototypes. The light reflections developed as I had envisioned, while the prototyping process allowed me to learn and refine the outcome along the way.

### III. Relevance of the Project and the Nordics
**Olga:** How might your design practice respond to the rhythms of seasons (like polar night or midnight sun) in future projects?

**Henri:** I like to refine and chase ideas a long way if they’re fruitful and create new avenues for deeper thought and expressions with different materials and techniques. I have a glass and light project called Northern Connection, where I have taken the light reflection patterns to more visually expressive direction with more advanced modular optic molds. There are two sculptural glass and light artworks combined with wood material, they are called The North Star and the Northern Lights and Arctic Sunset. I want to explore the northern nature themes and these techniques further. I believe my environment will always affect my creative practice.
![Photo by Olga K.](/assets/Publications/Henri4.jpg "Photo by Olga K.")

### IV. Tech, AI, Data & Ethics — Reflections & Experience
**Olga:** In a world where digital and physical sensibilities blur, how do you protect the authenticity of human touch in your objects?

**Henri:** The inspiration for my artistic concepts I design comes from my experiences and intuition, so the thought process behind the works is always human. My design and artworks are very much material based, so manufacturing and sculpting at a high-level needs craft skills and material understanding even when using advanced digital designing and fabricating tools, especially in the finishing and assembly phases of the works. Digital tools are useful and effective in design, but they are in assisting role in my design process.

### V. Vision & Future: Collaboration and Call to Action
**Olga:** If you could invite one unexpected collaborator (scientist, musician, poet, coder), who would it be and what question would you explore together?

**Henri:** If I could invite several unexpected collaborators, I would bring together experts from both technological and sensory fields. For immersive, programmable lighting systems, I’d collaborate with a creative coder or lighting engineer with deep expertise in light control, interactive environments, and the physical realization of installations. Together, we would explore light as a responsive, programmable material in space.
In my work combining audio and furniture design in a wellbeing context, I’d work with musicians, sound designers, and neuroscientists studying how sound affects the nervous system. I’m particularly interested in how low-frequency vibrations and rhythm can support relaxation when integrated into physical objects and environments.
I would also collaborate with nervous system specialists and embodiment practitioners to deepen the understanding of how multisensory design, combining light, sound, and tactile elements, can regulate the nervous system, reduce stress, and create immersive, restorative experiences.
![Photo by Olga K.](/assets/Publications/Henri6.jpg "Photo by Olga K.")

**Olga:** Which type of support (institutional, cultural, technological, community) would most amplify what you want to contribute next?

**Henri:** The type of support that would most amplify my next contribution is a combination of technological, institutional, and collaborative support. Funding and access to high-quality materials and advanced technologies are essential for developing and refining prototypes that integrate lighting, sound, and furniture design.
Community and cultural support also play a key role. Engaging with users, designers, and well-being practitioners allows the work to stay grounded in real human experiences while opening up new perspectives. Together, these forms of support create the conditions for a truly multidisciplinary design process, where lighting, sound, and furniture design can merge into innovative, research-driven solutions that promote well-being.

**Olga:** If your future work could act as a cultural bridge between design and another field (science, ecology, rhythm, sound), what would that bridge look like?

**Henri:** In my future work, this bridge would exist between design, sound, and neuroscience. It would take the form of multisensory environments and objects where furniture, light, and audio are integrated into tangible, experiential solutions that interact with the human body and mind. By combining ergonomic design with sound, vibration, and responsive technologies, these works could contribute to measurable wellbeing effects in everyday life.
At the same time, they could connect different fields of design, such as furniture, product, sound, and interaction design, with scientific research in a genuinely interdisciplinary way. I’m interested in how design can move beyond aesthetics to support wellbeing by creating moments of pause and recovery, and spaces where people can reconnect with themselves through sensory experience.
