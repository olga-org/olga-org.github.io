Why Most DEI Programs Fail—and How This Founder Is Doing It Differently

I. Origins & CEO’s Background

Olga K.: What early life or career experiences first made you aware of the intersections of identity, trauma and systemic injustice—and how have those shaped your decision to co-found Intersectional Healing Collective?

Anna Mäkinen: I think my awareness began less as an idea and more as a feeling - noticing how often people, including myself, were asked to shrink or translate parts of ourselves just to feel safe, and to belong. My understanding of how identity, injustice, and trauma intertwine unfolded over time, through witnessing what happens when people feel unseen or unsafe in spaces meant to support them.

I’ve always been drawn to community work, but I often felt the weight of navigating systems that weren’t designed with inclusive care in mind. That depletion revealed the demanding systems that weren’t offering enough listening, spaciousness, or room for people to show up as whole. These personal, cultural and societal experiences made me deeply curious about what healing could look like when it’s shared, relational, and rooted in community. Intersectional Healing Collective emerged from that curiosity and commitment to building cultures of care where everyone, regardless of background, can reconnect with each other.

Olga: Many people working in DEI start with institutional or academic backgrounds. How did your own path lead you toward a “trauma-conscious, healing” lens rather than only policy, compliance or training?

Anna: Policy, regulations and legal compliance are important, but after years in global DEI, I realized that change doesn’t sustain unless people feel safe enough to embody it. I didn’t want inclusion to be tokenistic and uprooted in the same harmful dynamics. I could say my path moved from “structure to soul” and from compliance to consciousness, and it became more about integrating accessible wellbeing practices into the DNA of culture transformation. 

Many of the challenges today, globally, aren’t just structural or individual, but they’re relational. They lie in the spaces of the humanity between us; in how we listen, repair, and make meaning together. Organizations, like people, carry different histories and synergies, and when those go unacknowledged, they show up as disconnection, defensiveness or fatigue. My work now is about creating the conditions where collective care can take root, as the foundation for a culture of care, restoring trust and belonging.

Olga: How do you maintain your own wellbeing, identity integrity, and growth, as someone leading work that often exposes you to heavy, difficult stories of injustice and trauma?

Anna: I’ve learned most of what I know about wellbeing by walking the talk. Lots of embodied practice, external support, and self-inquiry, pausing, reflecting, and noticing what nourishes me and my communities. Over time, I’ve learned to recenter joy and to stay connected to my values, as well as letting them guide my pace and how I lead. The work we do exposes a lot of pain, but it also reveals how antifragile we can be when we care for each other. I try to lead from a place of creating conditions of safety, reflection, and joy that ripple outward. I hold myself accountable, and I am getting better at pausing, accepting my own messiness, and approaching all feelings with curiosity too, as that is also necessary. When I’m aligned in that way, the culture we’re building has a chance to be, too.

 II. The Idea & Innovation of the Project You Develop

Olga: The IHC model describes things like “intersectional healing assessment,” “collective journeys,” and being “trauma conscious.” How do you design your programs so that healing isn’t just metaphorical, but delivers measurable change—for example in policies, power dynamics, or organizational culture?

Anna: Intersectional healing is both personal and structural,  so our programs are designed to bridge the inner and the outer. Each partnership begins with an assessment which helps us identify where disconnection, harm and systemic issues are showing (whether in policies, power dynamics, or the invisible culture of belonging). Our collective journeys, tailored processes, bring people together to practice new ways of relating, accountability, and trust. We track shifts in attitudes and mindsets, as well as tangible indicators in feedback flows, representation of influence, whose voices are centered in decision making, how conflict is addressed, and for example what kind of care is practiced in everyday operations. Our goal is to make healing visible in people's behaviors, not just in organizations’ language, and this can be measured using our Harm to Healing scale.

Olga:  What innovations in methodology (therapeutic, coaching, communal) have you introduced or adapted to make DEI more sustainable—and less prone to being superficial or performative?

Anna: We’ve woven together approaches from therapeutic, coaching, communal traditions and DEI, centering emotional intelligence, somatic awareness, and systems thinking in equal measure. Instead of one-off workshops, we design layered experiences like healing circles, reflection spaces, and peer learning labs which allow people to share and embody insight over time so change becomes sustainable. A big part of my work is inclusive leadership: supporting those in power to stay present and curious in discomfort, to build the empathy and relational capacity needed to hold real complexity.

Olga:  How do you balance risk: the risk of re-traumatization in organizational settings vs neglecting important but difficult conversations? What safeguards or design principles does IHC use?

Anna: There’s always a tension between moving too fast and not moving at all, taking on too much and avoiding what matters. I try to hold that tension with care and to design at the speed of safety. That means creating clear structures and agreements that can hold everyone, while staying flexible enough to respond to what arises in real time. Trauma-conscious frameworks help us stay attuned, recognize when overwhelm surfaces, and pause or repair when needed. We don’t avoid hard conversations as we are aware that avoidance is its own form of harm. We stand firmly against oppression and in unity with human dignity and rights. When people experience that difficulty can be met with respect, empathy, and accountability, they can begin to trust that change is possible.

III. Relevance of Finnish Startup & Innovation Ecosystem, and Your Initiatives

Olga: Finland is known for high trust societies and social welfare; yet also has its own struggles around inclusion for certain groups. How do Finnish cultural norms shape both opportunities and resistances for a project like IHC?

Anna: Finland’s culture of trust in social systems creates real opportunities for work like ours; there’s already an underlying belief at least that collective wellbeing matters. At the same time, there are certain circles, where that same harmony makes it difficult to name or address difference and discomfort.

Having just returned to Finland after more than ten years abroad (living and working across different countries and within teams that were richly multicultural and diverse), professional spaces here can feel quite homogenous. This contrast has given me perspective on both what Finland does remarkably well and where its growth edges lie. I love seeing most Finnish workplaces embracing the benefits of DEI now, but some of these modern places struggle to build the trust that would be possible, perhaps because they do often still depend on sameness? There are fears and cultural legacies from the past that are still often ignored, which can only be addressed - with compassion - if we rebuild trust from the foundation. Intersectional Healing Collective sits in that paradox. We build on my roots from here, Finland’s strengths of education, and the international awakening to the opportunity now; to help a high-functioning society become also a high-feeling one. Together we can create spaces where complexity, identity, and emotion aren’t seen as threats to cohesion but as pathways to deeper connection.

Olga:  As Head of Partnerships at Helsinki Pride and being embedded in Finnish startup / innovation contexts, how do you see synergies (or friction) between “social innovation” and “commercial innovation” when it comes to intersectional healing?

Anna: In Finland’s startup world, innovation often focuses on technology and efficiency, while social innovation asks how we relate and care. I see that they can and should be looked at as the same side of the coin. The future of innovation will be defined not only by what we build, but by how we build — and who it serves.  As Head of Partnerships at Helsinki Pride, I’ve seen firsthand how collaboration between civil society, public institutions, start ups and business can shift culture when values are shared and inclusion is an innovation strategy. Intersectional healing builds on that insight so that social and commercial innovation can come together.

Olga: What role (or potential role) do Finnish public institutions (municipalities, national government, health/social services) play in scaling or supporting what IHC does? Are there underused levers?

Anna: We see Intersectional Healing Collective as part of a broad innovation landscape. In Finland we work in partnership with both public and private sectors, offering frameworks and tools that help organizations translate values like equity and care into tangible practices. When public institutions model inclusive care and accountability, trust deepens; a governance win for the Finnish society. Rather than chasing growth for its own sake, we focus on depth and creating scalable models of relational trust that manifest across industries and borders.

Some examples:

1. Focused pilots with youth / employment services from IHC Assessment to Collective Journey to Practice Labs” with local staff and user populations.

2.  Occupational Health, inclusive wellbeing initiative & social services integration: Co-designed modules for front-line teams, embedded within existing training curricula.

3. Policy co-creation: cross-sector design workshops with leaders, service users, NGOs and practitioners co-creating service protocols rooted in harm-to-healing metrics (example of this Helsinki Pride editions).

IV. Tech, AI, Data & Ethics Reflections and Experience

Olga: How do you avoid the pitfall of “algorithmic inclusivity”—i.e. where tools claim to “solve bias” but embed new biases or flatten individual complexity?

Anna: One of the biggest risks in the field of improving diversity and inclusion is mistaking data processing for understanding people. I center people’s real experiences, the humanity of every story. Tools that promise to “solve bias” often end up encoding the worldview of whoever built them, flattening the nuance of lived experience and oversimplifying a very complex system such as the human experience. We are not trying to normalize harmful biases, but to challenge them, whilst recognizing that as humans, we are inherently operating with our biases, but we can learn to manage how they affect our decision making. At IHC, we design from a different place; tech must follow context, but not dictate it. Tech can enable us and make us more critical about our biases in decision making so that we, the humans, can make the human-centered choices. We co-create and co-design with the very communities affected, data is interpreted through relational processes, and qualitative nuance is always valued as are quantitative patterns. For us inclusivity is not something to automate, but to be built, by being relational, iterative and accountable.

Olga: In your experience, how do people respond to quantitative vs qualitative measurement in culture change / healing work? How do you balance narratives, lived experience and “hard metrics”?

Anna: People respond very differently depending on what they’ve been taught to trust, relate to and share. Some feel safer with numbers, others resonate with narrative, real stories, and some with embodied change. I see all that essential, because yes, the metrics show patterns and return on investment, but lived experience reveals meaning and authenticity. I like to use a dual-lens model, to track indicators like representation of voice and influence, conflict patterns, or psychological safety. With qualitative research I get insights from interviews, reflection circles, relational mapping and they show how and what people are actually experiencing. Belonging and healing only become real when the numbers and the experiences align. Our job is to hold both with integrity.

Olga: What data or tech innovations would you like to see (or build) in the next 3–5 years to make culture transformation, belonging, and healing more scalable while staying accountable and ethical?

Anna: I’m building tools that support relational work. In the next few years, I’d love to see and build more innovations that keep humanity at the center. I am open for co-creation and collaboration, and here are some of my ideas that together we can build on:

●       Sensing Culture with relational dashboards (like climate monitoring but culture micro-pulse). We already have a tool that measures Harm to Healing with anonymous, lightweight, frequent check-ins that track emotional climate, trust, and safety without surveillance. Think climate monitoring, but for relational patterns with intersectionality and wellbeing at the core, and not reporting just performance, but making flows of care, feedback, repair and inclusion visible.

●       Community-informed ethical models: Academically backed, strategic and practical frameworks where data interpretation involves humans from different identities and power positions. I want to find partners that keep us all accountable especially to those most impacted.

●       Somatic + qualitative tools that help individuals and teams track harmful patterns, not to diagnose, but to support early interventions.

V. Vision & Future of Your Projects

Olga:  If you imagine IHC five years from now, what kind of scale, impact, or institutional presence do you hope to have—and how will you know you’ve succeeded?

Anna: Five years from now, I hope Intersectional Healing Collective has continued to spread the message of collective care being the foundation for culture of care. We actually stand for something very simple and very human: to transform people to feel safe to be themselves, accountable to one another, and connected through real joy and wellbeing. I imagine us working across sectors and borders, in complexity and where the impact can be high; public institutions, health and social care, education, and global organizations navigating cultural tension. Our impact won’t be measured by scale alone, but by the depth of change: I want to continue meeting leaders who can hold discomfort within themselves, who disrupt harmful systems, and support teams who trust each other again, to choose care as a strategy.  The world and mindsets are shifting; climate instability, migration, polarization, digital noise and new innovations can all make belonging harder, but what it means for our work is that we will need to stay agile; we’ll keep focusing on repair, relational skills, emotional intelligence, and helping people find meaningfulness together. We’re already preparing for that and I know we can travel across different tensions without losing the ethical core. Success isn’t perfection but continuous development. I want to be able to look back and see that we helped organizations not just do things differently, but feel differently. I want to center safety, and reclaim joy as a form of resistance, to see accountability as a form of care, and healing as a practice that belongs to everyone. Finally, if I’m still learning, still listening, still growing alongside the people we serve, that’s when I’ll know I’ve done this work with integrity.
