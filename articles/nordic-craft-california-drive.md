# [Nordic Craft, California Drive: The Power of Two Worlds in One Project](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/nordic-craft-california-drive-power-two-worlds-one-olga-kryuchkova-pjg4f/?trackingId=bXOKx1ZwRgGDNGVOh6jtnQ%3D%3D)

*Published on 13-03-2026, 7 min read*

**Olga K.:** Mariia, please tell us about the Shader House founders’ background and how did you meet and how the project began?

**Mariia:** We met through an online startup networking space with Natasha Marashan and stayed in touch. It started as a digital kind of friendship — following each other, checking in occasionally, and supporting from a distance.

In early 2025, I joined a game jam at Espoo Game LAB (EGL2), invited to support a developer team specifically with marketing. My background is in IT and digital platforms — I’ve spent over 15 years building products and systems — and the gaming industry wasn’t something I had planned to enter. But during the jam I saw talented teams building incredible games — but struggling with visibility, validation, and structured feedback.
![Photo by Olga K.](/assets/Publications/Sh_House2.jpg "Photo by Olga K.")

When I shared those observations with Natasha, the topic immediately clicked. Natasha is a Business Strategy Manager in Los Angeles with a marketing background and an iMBA in digital marketing, and she’s deeply connected to games through her circle. She later came back with structured research that confirmed what we were seeing: this couldn’t be solved with one tool. It needed an ecosystem — and that’s how Shader House took shape.
![Photo by Olga K.](/assets/Publications/Sh_House1.jpg "Photo by Olga K.")

**Olga K.:** Mariia, you moved from systems engineering into indie games, what surprised you most about switching from structured enterprise tech to creative chaos?

**Mariia:** Honestly, I don’t experience indie games as “creative chaos.” From the inside it feels surprisingly structured, what looks chaotic from the outside is often a very organized creative process.

What truly surprised me was how much people can create in such a short time, and how deeply they care about what they’re building. In enterprise tech, work can become routine. Indie developers build entire worlds through code, atmosphere, story, emotion and that intensity is impressive and inspiring.

**Olga K.:** How did meeting your co-founder “on the other side of the world” shape the culture of Shader House from day one?

**Mariia:** We share the same cultural roots, which gives us a strong foundation of values and trust. At the same time, we’re both immigrants shaped by different countries. I’m based in Finland and influenced by Nordic and European ways of thinking: craft, sustainability, long-term quality. Natasha lives in California and brings an American mindset around visibility, speed, and market clarity.

Those differences don’t separate us, they complement each other. And the time-zone split became a strength: when one of us goes offline, the other one starts. Shader House was global by default from day one.
![Photo by Olga K.](/assets/Publications/Sh_House4.jpg "Photo by Olga K.")

**Olga K.:** What’s one early mistake or naive assumption you’re now grateful for because it changed your direction?

**Mariia:** At first, we thought the main issue was marketing. We assumed that better visibility alone would solve most problems, and that was naive.

Very quickly we realized what was missing was deeper: communication and human connection. The link between players and developers is often broken, and people don’t feel heard or seen. That insight shifted our direction from “promotion” to building infrastructure for interaction: structured feedback, community, trust, and discovery that benefits both sides.

**Olga K.:** If someone shadowed your team for a week, what would surprise them most about how you actually work together behind the scenes?

**Mariia:** Probably how much laughter there is. We joke a lot, and from the outside it might look chaotic. In reality it creates a safe space where even difficult things can be discussed openly.

We have a flat structure built on equality and respect. When there’s tension, we don’t pull in different directions — we talk it through and solve it. We’re relaxed in how we work, but extremely serious about what we deliver. We don’t expect perfection — we expect honesty, curiosity, and the willingness to grow.

**Olga K.:** Outside of tech and startups, what experiences have shaped your mindset as a founder?

**Mariia:** Outside of tech, I’ve spent my life in sport. I compete in dog agility and coach at an international level, including serving as a trainer for the Japanese national team at the World Championship. I’m also training for my first triathlon.

High-performance sport teaches resilience, psychological awareness, and structured preparation. It teaches you how to make decisions under pressure and how to keep moving when progress feels slow. Building a startup requires exactly the same mindset.

## II. The Idea & Innovation of Shader House

**Olga K.:** What is the problem Shader House solves — and what do you do differently or better than other platforms?

**Mariia:** Indie developers face a structural gap between:

- building the game
- marketing the game
- validating it before investing more time and money

Large studios have internal systems for this. Indies usually don’t.

Shader House focuses on this missing stage of the lifecycle — helping developers test ideas, gather structured feedback, and grow early visibility while their games evolve.

We don’t position ourselves as a replacement for large distribution platforms like Steam. Those platforms focus on large-scale distribution once a game is already prepared for market entry.

Platforms like itch.io made independent publishing accessible, which was an important step for the industry. But publishing alone doesn’t solve visibility or structured feedback.

Shader House focuses on what happens around the game: structured QA playtesting, meaningful interaction between developers and players, and visibility support — including free amplification of published games across our social channels. Our goal is simple: not just to host indie games, but to help them be discovered, improved, and supported by a real community. Building Shader House is an open invitation. If you believe indie games deserve better visibility, stronger communities, and real collaboration between developers and players — let’s build it together.

Shader House exists to become that missing layer: visibility and validation inside game design ecosystem.

We are not just a publishing page. We combine marketplace, community, structured QA playtesting, and transparent monetization into one workflow. Developers can share early builds in a closed QA space, create specific testing tasks, receive structured bug reports and suggestions, and continue building a community through devlogs and discussions.

And we care deeply about players, too. Many players genuinely want more indie games, but they don’t always know how to find them. Shader House is meant to be the bridge: helping players discover games they’ll love, and helping developers reach the right audience through real interaction, not noise.

![Photo by Olga K.](/assets/Publications/shsh.png "Photo by Olga K.")

When we went live on January 26, we kept expectations realistic. One month later we had 27 registered users (platform admin included), 10 developers, 16 players, and 2 games published by a real indie developer (one released, another one in QA playtest). Early numbers, but strong signals. Infrastructure always starts small.

![Photo by Olga K.](/assets/Publications/Sh_House5.jpg "Photo by Olga K.")

**Olga K.:** What problem in indie games made you personally frustrated enough to say “okay, I’ll build the solution myself”?

**Mariia:** What frustrated me most was seeing how many truly talented indie games never reach their players, not because they aren’t good, but because visibility becomes algorithm-driven and budget-driven.

On top of that, feedback and community are scattered across many platforms. Developers end up juggling Discord, forums, social media, spreadsheets, and random comments. I kept thinking: why is there no structured ecosystem where this lives together?

I’m not here to teach people how to make games. I’m here to build the stronger connective layer around games, so indie teams can validate earlier, reduce risk, and build visibility systematically instead of hoping to get lucky.

![Photo by Olga K.](/assets/Publications/Sh_House6.jpg "Photo by Olga K.")

**Olga K.:** What feature felt small when you built it but turned out to be unexpectedly powerful?

**Mariia:** Early QA playtesting before release.

We didn’t want “send feedback and disappear.” So we built a full loop: developers create testing tasks, testers submit structured reports with screenshots, and developers verify submissions (approve or reject). Both sides get notifications, and the process stays meaningful instead of becoming noise.

It also became unexpectedly powerful for testers: it can function as a portfolio. QA is a real profession, and being able to show what you tested and what you found — verified by real projects — is valuable.

**Olga K.:** How do you balance being a platform (neutral space) versus being an advocate (actively helping games grow)?

**Mariia:** We don’t separate neutrality and advocacy. For us, the platform itself is the act of advocacy.

Shader House is designed to support indie games by default. We don’t push specific genres or trends. We focus on people, trust, and structured interaction. And one key part of advocacy is visibility support: when a game is published on Shader House, we amplify it across our social channels (Instagram, TikTok, LinkedIn, and soon YouTube) for free, not as “promotion,” but as ecosystem support.

Indie teams often don’t have marketing departments. We want visibility to be systematic, not accidental.

**Olga K.:** If Shader House disappeared tomorrow, what would the indie community lose that they can’t easily replace elsewhere?

**Mariia:** They would lose an ecosystem built around real interaction and relationships — where discovery, community, early validation, and visibility support live in one place.

They would lose structured QA playtesting that helps games improve before release, and they would lose an advocate that supports indie visibility beyond the platform through free amplification. And players would lose a place designed to help them find indie games more easily — not just the “loudest” games, but the ones that truly match their taste.

## III. Relevance of the platform to Finnish and European Game Dev Needs

**Olga K.:** What’s uniquely “Nordic” or “European” about the indie ecosystem that global platforms often misunderstand?

**Mariia:** Nordic and broader European indie communities tend to be very product-focused. There’s a strong emphasis on craft, substance, and long-term quality. Teams often prioritize building something solid before talking about it. Collaboration matters, and the tone is often less hype-driven.

Global platforms often follow a different logic — speed, scaling, loud visibility. That approach isn’t wrong, but it doesn’t always align with how many European teams work. Shader House sits at that intersection: respecting European craft while building the visibility infrastructure that helps great games travel further.

**Olga K.:** Do smaller studios here struggle more with visibility, funding, or community, and which one are you secretly most obsessed with solving?

**Mariia:** In Finland and the Nordics, community and funding structures can actually be strong, there are grants, programs, workshops, and knowledge sharing. Where it gets harder is visibility beyond those circles.

That’s the part we’re most focused on solving: helping well-crafted indie games reach the right players internationally, and making visibility more systematic, through discovery inside the platform and free marketing support outside it.

**Olga K.:** How has participating in Espoo Game LAB or local jams shaped your understanding of what devs actually need vs. what founders think they need?

**Mariia:** EGL2 at Espoo Game LAB made one point very clear: game development can’t exist in isolation. It’s not only code and art, it’s also production, marketing, business strategy, and operations and shared since of belonging to inspiring community.

During the jam, it became obvious that while developers often feel they need “more jams,” what’s missing long-term is infrastructure: structured feedback loops, visibility support, and systems that help projects move forward after the jam ends.

**Olga K.:** If you could redesign one part of the European indie pipeline (education, publishing, funding, marketing), what would you rebuild from scratch?

**Mariia:** Marketing, but in a very specific way.

Europe already has strong education, funding support, and artistic identity. What could be strengthened is marketing through a more collective approach: shared visibility, common PR infrastructure, cross-promotion between teams, and collaboration instead of isolated competition for attention.

Shader House is one attempt to build that missing ecosystem layer in a way that fits indie workflows.

**Olga K.:** What signals tell you a developer is talented but just hasn’t been “seen” yet — and how can Shader House surface those hidden gems?

**Mariia:** Sometimes a game looks beautiful but doesn’t hold attention long. What stands out for us is “the spark”: mechanics that feel intentional, story that pulls you in, and an experience that creates presence.

To surface hidden gems, we focus on discovery through relevance and interaction — not pure spending power. The goal is to connect the right games with the right players, and let visibility grow from real engagement.

## IV. Tech, AI, Data & Ethics — reflections and experience

**Olga K.:** As a systems engineer, what technical decision in Shader House keeps you up at night because it will matter at scale?

**Mariia:** Trust at scale.

Community platforms can collapse quickly if safety, moderation, and transparency are weak. From early on, we focused on systems that protect trust: verification, anti-spam controls, reporting, admin tools, and workflows that keep interactions meaningful.

Scalability isn’t just traffic. It’s maintaining a healthy ecosystem as the platform grows.

**Olga K.:** How do you collect meaningful player feedback without turning developers into data slaves or overwhelming them with noise?

**Mariia:** We structure feedback so it stays actionable.

Developers can define tasks, testers submit reports with screenshots, bug severity, and clear categories (bugs vs suggestions). There’s a verification workflow so developers can approve, reject, and track progress — instead of drowning in random comments.

The goal is quality communication, not endless data.

**Olga K.:** Where do you draw the line between helpful AI and creativity-killing automation?

**Mariia:** AI should reduce busywork, not replace creative intent.

We see AI helping with things like summarizing feedback, translation, and spotting patterns — possibly even marketing assistance later. But the decision-making stays human. We want AI to strengthen communication and clarity, not automate creativity.

**Olga K.:** What ethical responsibility does a discovery platform have when its algorithms can decide which games succeed or disappear?

**Mariia:** A discovery platform has real ethical responsibility because algorithms shape opportunity.

In our view, that means not turning visibility into a paid advantage. The core should be personalization based on preferences and meaningful interaction — not only performance metrics or spending power. And algorithms shouldn’t become closed loops: there must be room for diversity and exploration, so players can discover games they didn’t already know to search for.

Our goal is not to “pick winners,” but to build a system that stays fair, open, and human.

**Olga K.:** If you had to choose: perfect data accuracy or human intuition from the community, which would you trust more, and why?

**Mariia:** We would trust human intuition from the community, not instead of data, but above it.

Data provides structure, but people give meaning. In many large ecosystems, decisions become overly metric-driven and the player’s voice gets lost. Shader House exists to rebuild that connection: hearing and seeing the end user, and creating open communication around games.

## V. Vision & Future: growth, collaboration, support

**Olga K.:** Imagine it’s five years from now and Shader House worked beyond your dreams — what changed for indie developers’ daily lives?

**Mariia:** Indie developers would spend less daily energy fighting for visibility and more energy making games.

They would validate earlier, reduce risk, and build community before release — not after a game disappears into silence. Visibility would become systematic, supported by discovery inside the platform and marketing support outside it.

**Olga K.:** What does “success” look like for you personally — scale, impact, or seeing one overlooked studio finally thrive?

**Mariia:** For us, success is impact.

If Shader House proves that the industry doesn’t have to rely only on monopolized, impersonal marketplaces — and that it’s possible to build spaces where developers are treated as humans, not content suppliers — that matters more than scale for its own sake.

If even one overlooked studio truly thrives because of that shift, that’s already real success.

**Olga K.:** If a small two-person team joins Shader House tomorrow, what journey do you hope they experience from day one with you?

**Mariia:** We want them to feel welcome from day one — ease, curiosity, and genuine excitement about sharing what they’re building.

We want them to feel they’re no longer working in isolation. They can build community through devlogs and discussions, involve players early through structured playtests, and grow visibility without needing big budgets. Most of all, they should feel heard — and supported in a way that respects long-term craft, not burnout.
![Photo by Olga K.](/assets/Publications/Sh_House10.jpg "Photo by Olga K.")

**Olga K.:** What’s one bold experiment you’d love to try with the platform if resources weren’t a constraint at all?

**Natasha:** Turning successful indie games into films, so strong stories and worlds can live beyond games and reach wider audiences.

**Mariia:** Building a personal assistant for developers and players, focused on marketing, learning, and decision-making, that helps translate feedback into clear priorities and practical next steps, without killing creative direction.

**Olga K.:** What kind of collaborator excites you most right now: investors, mentors, dev tools, publishers, or something unexpected?

**Mariia:** We’re open to different kinds of collaborators, but right now the most valuable are people who help us grow real traction in the right way: indie teams willing to publish and test with us, players and testers who want to shape games early, and communities or events that can bring the right people together.

We’re also looking for advisors and investors who understand indie ecosystems, but our immediate priority is feedback and real usage. Shader House grows strongest when it’s shaped together with developers and players. The more developers and players join Shader House, the more valuable the ecosystem becomes for everyone involved, creating a space where discovery, feedback, and community reinforce each other.
