# [What is mean, median, mode, standard deviation, standard error, sample variance and kurtosis.](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/what-mean-median-mode-olga-kryuchkova-qhl3f)

*Published on 2024-04-24 19:02*

Let's imagine we have some numbers:

5, 2, 6, 4, 2, 5, 7, 1, 5

Here's what each of those means:

1.  **Mean**: This is just another word for "**average**." Add up all the numbers and then divide by how many numbers there are. So, for our numbers: (5 + 2 + 6 + 4 + 2 + 5 + 7 + 1 + 5) ÷ 9 = 37 ÷ 9 = 4.11 (rounded to 2 decimal places). So, the mean is 4.11.
    
2.  **Median**: Imagine we line up all the numbers from smallest to biggest. The median is the **middle number**. If there are two middle numbers, we add them together and divide by 2. For our numbers: 1, 2, 2, 4, 5, 5, 5, 6, 7. There are 9 numbers, so the middle one is the 5th number, which is also 5. So, the median is 5.
    
3.  **Mode**: This is the number that **appears most often**. For our numbers the number 5 appears three times, which is more than any other number. So, the mode is 5.
    
4.  **Standard Deviation**: Imagine you have a bunch of numbers that represent how far apart each number is from the average (or mean). Standard deviation tells you how spread out those numbers are. Example: Let's say you have the numbers 3, 5, 7, 9, and 11. The average (mean) is 7. The standard deviation tells you how much each number differs from 7. It's like asking, "On average, how far away is each number from 7?" In this case, the standard deviation is 3.16.
    
5.  **Standard Error**: This one's a bit trickier. Imagine you have a group of friends, and you want to know how accurately their average height represents the average height of all kids in your school. Standard error tells you how much the average might vary from the true average of all kids in your school.
    
6.  **Sample Variance**: This one is about how much the numbers in a sample vary from each other. It's like asking, "How much do these numbers differ from each other?"
    
7.  **Kurtosis**: This measures how much the shape of a distribution deviates from the normal bell curve. Imagine you're making a graph of how many people are in each age group in a town. If most people are around the same age, and there are fewer people who are much younger or older, it will have low kurtosis. But if you have a lot of people at very young and very old ages, with fewer in the middle, it will have high kurtosis.
