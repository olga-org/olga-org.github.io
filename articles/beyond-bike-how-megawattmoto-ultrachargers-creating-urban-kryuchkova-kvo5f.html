I. Origins and Personal Journey

Olga K.: Val, what early experience or passion led you to pursue electric mobility and charging infrastructure?

Val Leinonen: It all started with personal experience: we had ridden motorcycles for many years and knew them inside out. When we began developing an electric motorcycle, we immediately encountered the main issue — the absence of fast and convenient charging infrastructure. Even the most advanced bikes lose their value if there’s nowhere to charge them. That’s when we realized the real breakthrough wasn’t just the vehicle itself, but the infrastructure behind it. UltraChargers was born as a direct response to this systemic problem — we are building charging solutions without which a sustainable ecosystem for light electric vehicles simply cannot exist.

Olga: Which mentor or event most significantly influenced your approach to building solutions in such technical fields?

Val: Two factors shaped us the most: our startup experience and constant contact with end users. We didn’t come from theory — we came from the streets, from real-world testing, from negotiations with sharing companies. It wasn’t so much mentors who inspired us, but conversations with operators telling us: “We want to deploy 100 electric bikes, but where do we charge them?” That direct feedback became our main source of insight and the driver of our practical, task-oriented approach.

Olga: How does your experience help you combine the roles of visionary and business strategist at UltraChargers and Megawattmoto?

Val: Our team covers all key areas: product, engineering, sales, legal, and finance. Each of us takes responsibility for a domain, which allows us not just to dream, but to execute. Megawattmoto serves as our testing ground — a real-world platform to validate user behavior and technology assumptions. UltraChargers, in turn, is a scalable infrastructure solution — the one that’s missing not only in Finland, but across Europe. Our strength lies in combining engineering vision with business logic.

Olga: In difficult moments, what philosophy do you rely on?

Val: Our internal mantra is: “There is no ecosystem without charging.” This is not a slogan but a strategy. When faced with doubt or tradeoffs, we ask ourselves: “Does this bring us closer to real, functioning infrastructure?” We avoid distractions and aren’t afraid to make tough calls. Sometimes it’s better to drop a feature than to compromise the foundation: reliability, compatibility, and speed.

II. The Idea and Innovation

Olga: UltraChargers focuses on fast charging for two-wheelers — what key insight led you to design a system specifically tailored to motorcycles and e-scooters?

Val: The key insight came from direct conversations with sharing operators and fleet managers. They all said the same thing: “Electric mopeds are great, but where can we charge them quickly?” We realized existing car charging infrastructure just doesn’t fit: it’s expensive, bulky, and incompatible. Wall sockets are simply too slow. That’s when the idea emerged: to create Europe’s first modular, scalable, fast-charging network tailored for light electric transport.

From the beginning, our mission has been to dramatically reduce operating costs for fleet owners and individual couriers. UltraChargers is not just about infrastructure — it’s about real-world savings: less downtime, fewer logistical delays, lower staffing and energy costs.

Olga: How do you distinguish innovation at UltraChargers from that of Megawattmoto? What synergies or tensions arise between these projects?

Val: UltraChargers and Megawattmoto function as two parts of the same ecosystem. Megawattmoto is our field lab: we use it to test charging behavior, battery performance, and power load responses. UltraChargers, meanwhile, is a scalable infrastructure product that can support dozens of models across the market. The synergy comes from designing both the vehicle and the charger in tandem. Naturally, some tensions arise — for example, between battery design and charging architecture — but these friction points often lead to real breakthroughs.

III. Finland and the Ecosystem

Olga: How do you see UltraChargers and Megawattmoto contributing to Finland’s mobility and green tech development — not just as products, but as part of a system?

Val: We’re not just building a company — we’re laying down the infrastructure for Finland’s new urban mobility. If CCS became the standard for EV fast charging, we aim for UltraChargers to become the equivalent standard for light electric vehicles: mopeds, scooters, and cargo bikes. Megawattmoto shows what this future can look like; UltraChargers makes that future possible today.

Olga: What gaps did you observe in the Finnish startup ecosystem when building your team and product?

Val: The most noticeable gap was the lack of specialization in light electric vehicles and infrastructure. Most of the attention still goes to electric cars. That said, Finland offers a great launch environment: stability, trust in technology, and access to grants.

Olga: Which partners are essential for scaling the UltraChargers and Megawattmoto ecosystem in Finland?

Val: City governments, energy providers, logistics operators, and mobility-as-a-service (MaaS) platforms are all critical. We offer more than hardware — we bring a full-stack solution that includes business modeling, logistics, and digital integration into the urban environment.

Olga: How does support from the Finnish government (e.g. Business Finland, grants) affect your scaling strategy?

Val: It allows us to move faster and more systematically. Government support lets us test hypotheses, launch pilots, and reduce risk. We just started to receive feedback and engage in dialogue with public programs, but we already see this as a key lever for international expansion.

Olga: How do you engage municipalities and public transit in integrating your infrastructure into the city?

Val: We don’t show up with a problem — we bring a solution. We present the economics, propose pilot programs, and speak in the language of sustainability and efficiency. UltraChargers is both environmentally sound and financially beneficial. That’s why it’s not just relevant — it’s necessary for modern cities.

IV. Technology, Data & Ethics

Olga: How do you approach data privacy and ownership in relation to charging and vehicle usage?

Val: All data is collected anonymously, transparently, and in compliance with GDPR. We don’t monetize data; we use it to improve our services — like optimizing routing or charging sessions.

Olga: How do you balance rapid innovation with ethical design?

Val: If something can’t be made safe, we don’t release it. Certification, independent testing, and user protection are our top priorities. We’re not building a startup for tomorrow — we’re building infrastructure for the next decade.

Olga:. What sustainability metrics do you track?

Val: We track the following metrics- in our case:

  • Total energy consumption

  • Percentage of renewable energy use

  • CO₂ emission reductions

  • Battery lifecycle performance

  • Recycling efficiency We’re ready to report these metrics openly.

V. Vision and the Future

Olga: What will success look like for your companies in 5–10 years?

Val: The UltraChargers network will be a core enabler of urban mobility in Europe. Our ultimate goal is to build an ecosystem where charging, transportation, and energy are all interconnected.

Olga: How do you envision the evolution of integration between vehicles and charging infrastructure?

Val: Charging will become smart, automatic, and fully integrated. UltraChargers will be the “entry point” into the city’s energy network, communicating with both vehicles and infrastructure in real time.

23. If you had unlimited capital, what bold idea would you pursue?

Val: We would build a global network, establish a unified charging and battery standard, and deploy a hybrid system of energy, logistics, and transport available on every city block.

Olga: How can UltraChargers and Megawattmoto help position Finland as a global leader in light electric mobility?

Val: Finland can become the first country where light electric mobility becomes the norm rather than the exception. We aim to export not just the technology, but the mindset: clean, intelligent, and accessible by design.

Olga: What user or market insight could radically change your business model?

Val: If vehicle ownership disappears and everything becomes on-demand, we are ready. UltraChargers could evolve into a hub for logistics, rental, energy, and smart urban navigation. We’re building a flexible system that’s future-proof by design.
