# [“Namaste Helsinki”: What a Nepali Startup Summit Taught Me About Global Entrepreneurship 🌍](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/namaste-helsinki-what-nepali-startup-summit-taught-me-olga-kryuchkova-sprcf/?trackingId=bXOKx1ZwRgGDNGVOh6jtnQ%3D%3D)

*Published on 15-03-2026, 6 min read*

Namaste नमस्ते - “I respect you.” 🙏 That’s how I was welcomed at the Bato Summit, and honestly, this welcome word set the tone for the entire event, that was organized by the Nepali startup community at the vibrant startup hub Maria 01 in Helsinki.

## First impressions
I’ll admit: before arriving, I had a small concern: would this be a very niche community gathering mainly for Nepalis? Two minutes after entering the hall, that assumption quietly packed its bags and left. The room was full of people from different countries, industries, and backgrounds. Founders, investors, students, ecosystem builders, the usual suspects of the Helsinki startup world. The summit was clearly international in spirit, and Nepali entrepreneurship was its heartbeat.

Registration. Badges and band wrists - traditional conference's routine. And a surprisingly soft, almost philosophical cup of coffee courtesy of AromaNepal. The program was intense and inspiring: 3 keynotes, 2 panel discussions, 10 startups showcase, networking, and even an afterparty. 🚀 Here are a few moments that stood out for me:

## Building startups that actually solve real problems
One of the keynote speakers, Nima Sophia Tisdall, Managing Partner at Nordic Markets, shared the story of building a startup designed to support small-scale fishermen in Denmark.

![Photo by Olga K.](/assets/Publications/Namaste1.jpg "Photo by Olga K.")

Her idea focuses on improving supply chains and empowering small producers, something that immediately made me think of Finland and her agricultural sector. Imagine a similar model here:

- shortening supply chains for local farms
- enabling direct distribution
- supporting local food self-sufficiency
- and potentially lowering food prices

A startup like that could become a serious catalyst for regional agriculture in Finland. Sometimes innovation isn’t about inventing something entirely new, it’s about applying the right idea in the right place.

## Integration of international talent in Finland: the honest conversation
One of the most engaging discussions of the evening featured:

- Bijay Baniya, Founder of SisuCare
- Bijay Gurung, Founder of BGPLAY Capital
- Barun Bashyal, Founder of Resimator
- Prakash Pant, Founder & Partner, NORDES

![Photo by Olga K.](/assets/Publications/Namaste2.png "Photo by Olga K.")

These are highly educated international professionals who have been working in Finland for decades. The discussion touched on a topic that always sparks interest: How well does Finland actually integrate international talent? Not the official speeches version, the real version. The panelists were honest, but optimistic. Their key message: Yes, there are challenges. Yes, bureaucracy exists. But international entrepreneurship in Finland absolutely makes sense.

One practical recommendation stood out: 👉 Build multicultural teams. And even more practical: 👉 Include at least one Finnish co-founder. Not just for cultural reasons, but because it can significantly simplify bureaucratic and institutional processes.

## A lunch break worth mentioning
The lunch break deserves its own paragraph. A beautifully prepared three-course Nepali meal: light, aromatic, and surprisingly energizing. Startup events usually offer pizza or sandwiches. This one offered a culinary journey to Nepal.

## The talk that stole the show
After lunch came what was, for me, the highlight of the summit. A keynote by Apurva Ganoo, Lecturer in Entrepreneurship at Aalto University. Some speakers deliver information. Some speakers deliver motivation. Apurva delivers ignition. His talk touched on ideas inspired by Steve Jobs and focused on themes every founder should probably tattoo on their wall:

- There is no age limit to making a difference
- The power of underdogs
- Persistent work builds resilience
- Big ideas require unreasonable belief
- The power of teams

![Photo by Olga K.](/assets/Publications/Namaste3.png "Photo by Olga K.")

At some point during the talk I caught myself thinking: I should probably take his entrepreneurship course. Because when a lecturer can inspire a room full of founders, you know the material is good.

## Startup Pitch Showcase
Then came the adrenaline part. 10 Nepali founders pitched only for 3 min their startups to a jury who evaluated each pitch. They asked challenging questions, and shared constructive feedback on the business idea, market validation, scalability, and funding readiness. Jury includes Nima Sophia Tisdall (Managing Partner at Nordic Makers) and Tarun Sharma (Partner & COO at Nokia Ventures).

## Final Panel: The Nordic Startup Ecosystem
The final discussion focused on the future of the Nordic startup ecosystem and featured: Sarita Runeberg (CEO, Maria01), Monika Liikamaa (Co-Founder, Enfuce), Jussi Kallasvuo (General Partner, Antler) and Bikash Gurung (Founder, Bato). The conversation reinforced something many founders already feel: the Nordics remain one of the most supportive startup ecosystems in the world, but competition is global, and founders need to think internationally from day one.
![Photo by Olga K.](/assets/Publications/Namaste6.png "Photo by Olga K.")


Final key speaker Pouria Kay, Founding Partner & Chief Venture Officer at Atlan shared how to build a fundable startup. Which, as any founder knows, is slightly different from simply building a startup.


## The afterparty
Cozy. Informal. A smaller circle of founders and organizers. Exactly the kind of atmosphere where the best conversations actually happen.

## My 3 takeaways from the Bato Summit
1️⃣ Dream bigger than your first success. Your first milestone is not the destination, it's the starting point.
2️⃣ Multicultural teams are a superpower. Diverse perspectives create stronger startups.
3️⃣ The most valuable startup communities are the welcoming ones. And the Nepali community proved exactly that.
