# [“Creativity Is a System, Not Magic”: Inside Rovio Producer Aleksandr Dadaev’s Playbook](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/creativity-system-magic-inside-rovio-producer-dadaevs-olga-kryuchkova-3i4ef)

*Published on 2026-02-09 15:00*

### I. Origins & Background, Games Marketing Roots

Olga K.: Aleksandr, please tell us a bit about your background and how did you find yourself in Finland? What did the country bring to the table, and what difficulties did you encounter, and how did you overcome them?

Aleksandr Dadaev: I was born in Saint Petersburg, where I met my future wife and landed my first serious job. Later, we lived in Minsk and Moscow, building our careers and even taking out a mortgage. When the war began, the company I worked for relocated out of Russia and transferred its employees. We moved to Cyprus, where we had lived for 2 years. After that, I was offered a position in Finland, which is how we ended up here. We have always been curious about living in different places, and Helsinki felt almost like home, because of its proximity to Saint Petersburg. I had visited the city several times as a student, though usually only for a day or two at a time.

**Olga:** Before entering game marketing, was there a *single cultural experience* (book, game, or film) that shaped how you define “creativity” in interactive media?

**Aleksandr:** *The Lord of the Rings*. Both the books and the films showed me how entire worlds and languages can be built, with deep, complex characters and stories so compelling that you want to return to them again and again. How cinematographers reinvent many tools for specific shots, and how much mastery is involved. I am a big fan of the series and have reread *The Silmarillion* several times.

**Olga:** How did your first indie project (like *Scary Match*, which you shared publicly) influence your approach to team production later in AAA environments (big-budget, high-profile, large-team)? 

**Aleksandr:** My first personal project actually came after I had already worked on large-scale titles, born from an urge to create something of my own. I think everyone in the games industry dreams of making their "ideal" game—it’s an inherently creative field.

**Olga:** If you were to design a “career path simulation” game about your own professional journey, what would be the biggest plot twist?

**Aleksandr:** Moving to Cyprus was an unexpected and fascinating experience that I will remember for the rest of my life. My grandfather had also spent several years on the island in his time—he worked as a doctor in Cuba as part of a Soviet delegation in the 1970s—and I find it meaningful that our experiences resonate with each other in such an unexpected way.

**Olga:** Which life skill — unrelated to games — has driven your work ethic most in the studios you’ve been part of?

**Aleksandr:** The ability to genuinely care about the outcome. It’s important to aim for value and to constantly look for ways to improve both the final result and the process behind it.

### II. The Idea & Innovation in Current Game Marketing Approach

**Olga:** You wrote about the role of a *creative producer in mobile gaming* — what’s one misconception about the role that actually leads to *breakthrough creative decisions*? 

**Aleksandr:** Creativity is less about "inspiration" and more about having a system. Talent alone is not enough to stay in the industry long-term or achieve real success. Creativity works like a muscle: without regular training, it won’t develop. Only after years of consistent practice do you start to see truly strong, reliable results.

**Olga:** If creative innovation in games were a language, which linguistic concept (e.g., grammar, dialect, metaphor) best describes how you speak it?

**Aleksandr:** Grammar. Innovation rarely emerges on its own; more often, it is the result of a clear system and repetition.

**Olga:** In your experience, what’s a seemingly trivial creative practice that ends up producing *the biggest player attachment*?

**Aleksandr:** Visual style. A game’s unique look can continue to attract players long-term; they are often willing to invest in an aesthetic even when the core mechanics are relatively conventional.

**Olga:** How do you balance *artistic intuition* vs *data insights* when they point in opposite directions on a project?

**Aleksandr:** Constraints stimulate creativity, which means data can actually help guide the process. In some ways, it’s easier to develop a strong idea based on an example that is already working for you or a competitor. Creating a breakthrough concept from scratch is much harder. Even then, our ideas are shaped by prior experience, so in a sense, all creativity is data-driven—a recombination of existing ideas into a new form.

**Olga:** If you had to remove one traditional step in the creative development pipeline forever, what would it be — and why?

**Aleksandr:** The manual process of uploading to ad networks and testing. It would be a dream come true if that entire stage were fully automated.

###  III. Game Dev, Finnish & International Markets

**Olga:** What’s been the biggest *cultural learning* from working in both Finnish and global game studios that most teams overlook?

**Aleksandr:** The importance of a personal connection with colleagues. We spend a huge portion of our lives at work, yet we often barely know the people next to us. I believe it’s always beneficial to grab a beer after work once in a while and talk about personal interests—hobbies, pet projects, and life outside the office.

**Olga:** How would you explain the difference between *Finnish players’ expectations* and those of players in other major markets?

**Aleksandr:** There aren’t many major differences. The gaming market is global, so player behavior is fairly universal. Of course, some regions prefer hardcore MMOs while others lean toward casual, relaxing farming games, but these categories exist everywhere.

**Olga:** As someone who’s worked across markets, what’s a *local trend* you think global publishers should pay more attention to?

**Aleksandr:** AI-driven workflows. I believe we will soon see "unicorn" companies consisting of only 1–3 highly skilled professionals using a sophisticated, custom AI workflow.

**Olga:** If you could build a *cross-border game dev exchange program* between Finland and another region, what would it focus on?

**Aleksandr:** It should focus on work culture and communication. Many companies could learn from the Finnish "people-first" approach and work-life balance. Conversely, Finnish teams could benefit from observing the passion, rapid decision-making, and bold solutions found in other regions.

**Olga:** What’s one area where the Finnish mobile game ecosystem excels — and one where it still has something to *prove* internationally?

**Aleksandr:** Finnish work culture is amazing in its care for personal development and ethical communication. However, decision-making can often be very slow. It is sometimes challenging to try new tech or launch bold experiments. The world changes fast, and we must adapt just as quickly.

### IV. Tech, AI, Data & Ethics

**Olga:** With AI increasingly shaping creative workflows, what’s one *unique creative role* you believe must stay human-led?

**Aleksandr:** Creativity and Strategy are unmatched human skills. AI can solve many problems, but it doesn't know *which* problems to solve first. As Jorge Ramírez Carrasco a former colleague of mine put it: "What matters even more now is understanding the whole picture—defining what to build, why it should exist, and how it all fits together." ([link](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/jorgeramirezcarrasco_the-kind-of-engineering-work-i-was-doing-activity-7423942010945961984-aVNQ?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAAfqZYgBeZ54zOtQqj4LZLc5YzFXar2V3Os))

**Olga:** What’s a specific ethical dilemma you’ve faced when integrating data-driven decisions into creative production?

**Aleksandr:** Marketing can be quite cynical. Sometimes, what is best for the business looks "janky" or doesn't match style guides. We often have to face the reality that Quality does not always equal ROI (Return on Investment).

**Olga:** Imagine you could train an AI solely on gaming *failures* — what insights would you hope it learns first?

**Aleksandr:** That would be awesome. If we can learn exactly what *doesn't* work, it makes our lives much easier. As Thomas Edison famously said, “I have not failed. I've just found 10,000 ways that won't work.”

**Olga:** As data becomes central to live ops and UA, how do you guard against *creative homogenization* across titles?

**Aleksandr:** I don’t think it’s a huge problem, data has been central to UA for many years now. A lot of games are similar, yet they have unique features that attract players. When players get bored, they naturally seek out something fresh. User Acquisition is constantly evolving; when a trend dies, marketers react immediately to find the next one. It’s simply a matter of reaction speed.

**Olga:** If tomorrow a major AI could *co-write storylines* with your team, what limits would you impose on it?

**Aleksandr:** I actually plan to work on a system like that. I would want to guide it: I provide the fabula, the main beats, and the structure, and then I select the best pieces the AI generates. AI should work as an assistant—many great writers have used assistants throughout history anyway.

###  V. Vision & Future of Projects, Collaboration, Support

**Olga:** What’s the one creative gap in current mobile games that you’re most excited to explore — and why does it matter to you?

**Aleksandr:** I believe advanced, custom AI tools will help us create better games more affordably. While the quality bar should remain high, I want to focus more on fresh ideas, captivating stories, and new visual languages rather than just the "grind" of execution.

**Olga:** If you could invent a *new form of collaboration* between game studios and non-gaming creative industries (e.g., film, music), what would it look like?

**Aleksandr:** A hybrid of TV series and games. Many great games borrow from the episodic format—*Alan Wake* by Remedy is a perfect example. I’m looking forward to a deeper convergence of these two mediums.

**Olga:** What kind of partnerships do you think could *radically accelerate* Rovio’s next creative chapter?

**Aleksandr:** I think working on a hardcore, "Souls-like" mobile game could be a game-changer. There is a common misconception that mobile gamers only want casual experiences. However, projects like *Level Devil* or *Brawl Stars* prove there is a massive audience for games that require skill and focus. I’d love to see a hardcore puzzle game from Rovio.

**Olga:** What’s a future trend you believe will redefine how players *emotionally connect* with games?

**Aleksandr:** Unique storytelling will always be an evergreen trend. Even in the age of short-form content, long-form streaming shows still capture people for hours. Yes, we might get distracted while watching, but the story still has to be good. As technology is democratized, the value of a truly good story will only increase.

**Olga:** What type of support — from talent, tech, or community — would enable you to take your creative vision further than ever before?

**Aleksandr:** Investment and Visa Support: Access to a streamlined mechanism for securing both financial backing—such as a grant—and spousal visa support would be a significant catalyst for growth. I am eager to connect with anyone who can share insights or existing resources regarding such integrated instruments.
