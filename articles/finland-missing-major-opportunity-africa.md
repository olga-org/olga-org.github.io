# Why Finland Is Missing a Major Opportunity in Africa, And What Needs to Change Now

### What happens when powerful ideas meet even limited visibility?

On April 12, 2026, I had the privilege of moderating Panel 3 Law, Business & Policy Leadership at the Nordic African Business Forum. The Forum was opened with the keynote address [Lukumanu Iddrisu](https://www.linkedin.com/in/lukumanuiddrisu/). He elevated many important topics, but the main focus was on building new partnership. Because new challenges of the time require new approaches to solving problems and uniting joint efforts.
![Nordic African Business Forum.](/assets/Publications/africa1.jpeg "Nordic African Business Forum")
It was, without exaggeration, a spectacular event. And yet, I walked away with two conflicting emotions. On one hand, genuine inspiration. The kind that comes from meaningful dialogue, shared ambition, and the sense that something important is beginning to take shape. On the other, a sense of missed opportunity. Because despite the depth and relevance of the discussions, the forum did not receive the level of attention and engagement it truly deserved from the Finnish side.
![Nordic African Business Forum](/assets/Publications/africa2.jpeg "Nordic African Business Forum")

Yes, Finnish companies were present. Yes, they actively participated. But there should have been more.
Because events like this are not just conversations, they are catalysts for long-term economic cooperation between the Nordic and African regions.
![Nordic African Business Forum.](/assets/Publications/africa3.jpg "Nordic African Business Forum")

### What the Experts Made Clear
The discussion was grounded, honest, and at times uncomfortable. Which is exactly what made it valuable.

**Nora Haapala, Attorney-at-law, Associate Partner (Finland)** commented:
”From a business perspective, Finland–Africa collaboration slows down when rules are unclear, timelines are uncertain and partnerships stay too short‑term. I see strong demand and real opportunities, but companies hesitate when they can’t assess regulatory processes or find reliable long‑term partners. The shift we need is straightforward: treat African markets as strategic, not experimental - involve African partners early as decision‑makers and use existing assets, like diaspora expertise and local professional networks, much more deliberately. That’s how cooperation becomes practical, trusted and scalable.”

**Lorraine Otieno Global Talent Acquisition, Beyond the Savannah, (Kenya)** she emphasized in her speech:
”In my view, the biggest gap in conversations around law, business, and policy leadership is not vision, but execution. We continue to speak about opportunity, yet access remains uneven because systems are not designed to work for the talent they aim to serve. Africa is not short of capability, it is short of visibility, alignment, and intentional pathways into global markets. If we are serious about Finland–Africa collaboration, we need to move beyond well-crafted strategies and build practical, enabling frameworks that recognize skills, accelerate mobility, and treat African professionals as equal contributors not participants. Anything less simply reinforces the status quo.”

**Alhagie Jabbi, Assistant Chief Executive, Ceci Educare Oy, (Finland, Gambia)** formulated:
”My emphasis was for the African diaspora to have a voice in Finland and the Nordic by extension, and there is a need for an umbrella organisation of the African diaspora in Finland. For the African diaspora voice to be heard, it is high time we speak with one voice under one organisation”

The Nordic African Business Forum was not just an event. It was a signal — clear, timely, and impossible to ignore for those paying attention. If we step back and look at the bigger picture, three key takeaways emerge:
1. Strategy is no longer the problem, execution is. We are not lacking ideas, frameworks, or ambition. What is missing are practical mechanisms that turn intent into action, and partnerships into long-term results.
2. Equal partnership must replace outdated models. The shift from aid-based thinking to co-creation is not optional anymore. Sustainable collaboration will only happen when African partners are involved as decision-makers from the very beginning.
3. Diaspora is an underutilized strategic asset. With a growing and highly capable African diaspora in Finland, there is a unique opportunity to bridge markets, cultures, and systems if we choose to engage it intentionally and structurally.

This is where the real work begins.
Because forums like this should not end as conversations, they should evolve into commitments, collaborations, and measurable outcomes. The potential is there. The talent is there. The momentum is building. The question is who is ready to act on it?
